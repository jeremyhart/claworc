### [W6.23] Accountability structures in ADHD coaching — Confidence: high

ADHD coaching treats **external accountability as a core therapeutic mechanism**, not an add-on: it compensates for the executive-function deficits (working memory, self-monitoring, follow-through, time blindness) that make self-directed plans collapse between sessions. The professional definition makes this explicit — coaches "provide the structure and accountability that support clients as they work towards their self-identified goals," and access to the coach between sessions "bolsters client accountability and engagement" [S1].

**Session cadence.** Weekly is the default and near-universal standard. In the largest review of the field, *all* studies of college-student coaching used a model of **weekly sessions**; intervention length ranged from **8 to 24 weekly sessions** [S1]. Common formats: ~8 weeks (Prevatt & Yelland) up to 12–24 weeks; sessions typically 30–60 minutes, often phone/video so geography is irrelevant [S1][S2][S4]. In practice coaches start weekly (sometimes twice-weekly in crisis) and taper to biweekly only as a client's own systems stabilize [S3].

**Between-session check-ins.** A defining feature. Coaches "invite clients to do a brief check-in" between regular sessions "via phone, text, email, or face-to-face, at regular intervals or on an as-needed basis," often at no extra charge [S3]. A widely used tactic is **"book-ending"** — a quick contact immediately *before* and *after* a dreaded or emotionally loaded task to reduce avoidance and confirm completion [S3]. Purpose is momentum and connection to insight, not surveillance [S3].

**Between-session assignments (BSAs).** The accountable unit of work. Tasks are co-designed, concrete, and small (e.g., "buy a planner," "schedule daily study times") [S1]. Evidence specifically tying accountability to results: BSA quality matters — when coaches rated client **compliance with, time spent on, and quality of** the BSA more highly, clients showed greater gains in anxiety, concentration, selecting main ideas, and test strategies [S1]. Written (vs. oral) assignments correlated with more time on task (r = .71, p < .01), and coach-rated progress tracked client attitude toward BSAs (r = .84, p < .01) [S1]. Implication: write the action down, make it specific, and review it.

**What coaches track.** Across the practice literature the recurring objects of accountability are: (1) **self-identified goals** and progress toward them; (2) **specific action steps / next actions** with due dates; (3) **obstacles** (distinguishing emotional overwhelm from structural overload); (4) **wins and follow-through**; and (5) **reminder/self-monitoring systems** built to run between sessions [S1][S2][S3]. The PAAC Action competencies name this cluster directly — "Priorities • Structure • Action • Accountability • Learning," including planning, goal-setting, due dates, requests, challenges, **body-doubling**, initiating, sustaining, and follow-through [S2].

**Accountability formats to offer.** Body-double / co-working blocks; scheduled midweek text or email check-ins; book-ending around hard tasks; written action lists reviewed at the next session; external reminder systems and timers; and explicit progress evaluation against the client's stated goals [S1][S2][S3]. Effectiveness rests on a strong, nonjudgmental partnership — relationship quality, collaborative goal-setting, empathy, and instilling hope are the factors most consistently linked to good outcomes [S1][S3].

**Evidence quality.** The Field et al. (2013) RCT of college coaching found significantly improved executive functioning (LASSI total d = 1.02; Will/Skill/Self-Regulation d = 0.65–1.10) versus controls — supporting the structured weekly + between-session model, though most studies are small [S1].

### [W6.24] Handling resistance, avoidance & shame — Confidence: high

**The shame frame (why this is the core lever)**
Hallowell calls shame "the single most debilitating part of having ADHD," arguing that years of negative feedback for behaviors one couldn't control leave a residue that "raises your stress hormone levels and eventually corrodes your memory and executive functions" [S1]. Treat this as an expert clinical thesis, not a measured statistic — but it is well-supported in spirit: in a controlled study of 543 adults with ADHD vs. 313 without, the ADHD group scored markedly lower on self-compassion (M=2.57 vs 3.35, p<.01), and low self-compassion partially *mediated* their higher depression/anxiety/stress and lower wellbeing [S2]. Implication for Karlie: self-criticism isn't a side issue, it's a maintaining mechanism. Raising self-compassion is a legitimate coaching target, not soft talk.

**Separate shame from guilt; build shame resilience**
Brown's distinction is the practical hinge: guilt = "I did something bad" (behavior, workable); shame = "I am bad" (identity, paralyzing) [S3]. Coach toward guilt-level, behavior-specific framing ("the system failed, not you"). Brown's four shame-resilience elements give Karlie concrete moves: (1) help the client *recognize* shame and its physical/trigger cues, (2) build *critical awareness* of unrealistic norms ("a neurotypical-paced plan"), (3) prompt *reaching out* — shame dies in connection, and (4) *speaking shame* aloud rather than hiding the missed week [S3]. Empathy is the active ingredient: "empathy creates a hostile environment for shame" [S3]. Hallowell independently names connection as his "#1 treatment for ADHD" [S4].

**Avoid the coach-as-nag trap (MI core)**
The instinct to correct and push — the "righting reflex" — backfires: "If you try to prove a point, the client predictably takes the opposite side," producing *sustain talk* and relationship *discord* [S5]. For a population already primed for shame, nagging re-enacts the critical authority figures that caused the wound. Use the MI spirit (PACE): **P**artnership (client drives), **A**cceptance (autonomy + affirmation of worth), **C**ompassion, **E**vocation of the client's *own* reasons to change [S5]. Lean on OARS — open questions, affirmations, reflective listening, summaries — and when discord rises, "change direction or listen more carefully" rather than arguing [S5].

**Work with non-adherence and avoidance, not against it**
- Reframe a skipped task as data ("what got in the way?"), not a verdict. Roll with resistance; evoke change talk instead of supplying it.
- Calibrate evidence honestly: adding MI to behavior therapy in the community STAND RCT (n=278 teens) did **not** beat usual care on symptom reduction in intent-to-treat analysis — but it significantly improved *engagement* (parent session attendance 74% vs 46%, d=0.88) and medication follow-through [S6]. Takeaway: MI's reliable payoff is buy-in and retention — exactly the resistance/avoidance problem — more than symptom change on its own.

**Normalize non-linear progress**
Explicitly pre-frame relapse and plateaus as expected. Pair affirmation of effort/worth with behavior-specific guilt-level problem-solving, and surround the client with people "who see more good in you than you see in yourself" [S1]. Setback ≠ failure; it is the shape of the work.

### [W6.25] Body doubling & external accountability — Confidence: medium

**What body doubling is.** Body doubling is the practice of using another person's *social presence* — co-located or remote, live or recorded, a friend or a stranger — to help start, sustain focus on, or finish a task; the body double typically works on their own activity rather than directly assisting [S1][S2]. The community itself defined it this way in the first formal study, and many neurodivergent people reported having done it for years before learning the term [S1]. CHADD frames it as a form of *accountability partner* technique done "in the same room or virtually through videochat platforms" [S3].

**How it is used (coach-facing).** Practical, structured implementations work best: fixed timeboxes (e.g., 50-minute sessions), a clear single task chosen *before* the session, an optional brief check-in at start/end, and platforms like virtual co-working communities for remote pairing [S3]. Stated mechanisms include accountability, a "visual reminder to remain on task," reduced isolation/overwhelm, behavioral momentum, and sustained attention [S1][S2]. Caution clients on two documented failure modes: drifting into socializing instead of working, and over-reliance that crowds out building independent task-initiation skills [S3].

**ACTUAL evidence status — be honest.** The evidence is **emerging and largely self-reported, not established.**
- *Self-report is positive:* Eagle et al. surveyed 410 respondents, 220 completed (87.7% neurodivergent; ADHD n≈139), and ~85% said body doubling significantly helped them complete tasks [S1]. This is observational/survey data, not a controlled trial.
- *Controlled experiments have NOT shown an effect:* A 2025 design paper's literature review states plainly that "no studies to date have clearly demonstrated the effectiveness of body doubling, and existing findings remain inconclusive," and that controlled studies (including ones with ADHD participants) "show no significant effect of body doubling support, even if participants perceived them as motivating" [S2].
- *No clinical-guideline endorsement:* Body doubling does not appear in NICE/CADDRA/AAP guidance; treat it as a low-risk, plausibly-helpful practitioner technique, not an evidence-based intervention.
- *A real trade-off:* Being observed can *raise* anxiety and even trigger task avoidance in some people, so it is not universally helpful [S2].

**Other external-accountability mechanisms (better-evidenced).**
- *Written goals + progress reports to another person:* In Matthews' study, participants who wrote goals, committed to actions, AND sent weekly progress reports to a friend reached ~76% goal accomplishment (achieved or at least halfway), vs ~43% for those with unwritten goals — a frequently cited demonstration that accountability + commitment beats intention alone [S4]. (Single study, business goals, not ADHD-specific.)
- *Financial stakes / commitment devices (e.g., StickK-style contracts):* A randomized controlled trial of a financial commitment product (CARES) found 11% take-up, and those offered it were ~3 percentage points more likely to pass a 6-month nicotine test than controls, persisting at 12 months — a real but modest effect, contingent on people choosing to stake money [S5]. Vendor-reported success rates (e.g., "78% with stakes + referee") are marketing figures and are **reported but unverified** here.

### [W6.26] Goal-setting & follow-through systems for ADHD — Confidence: high

**Frame ADHD as a performance disorder, not a knowledge disorder.** Barkley characterizes ADHD as an impairment of executive function and self-regulation: the problem is not *knowing what to do* but *doing what one knows* at the point of performance [S1]. Self-directed motivation toward delayed goals is weak, so behavior is captured by the immediate context rather than long-term consequences [S1]. Solanto's clinical model echoes this: medication alone "may not be sufficient... and explicit skills training in adulthood may be necessary" [S2]. **Coaching implication:** design the environment to do the regulating; don't rely on the client to "try harder" to remember or care later.

**Externalize the goal and its cues.** Because internal working memory and self-motivation are unreliable, goals and time must be made physical and visible. NICE NG87 formally recommends *environmental modifications* — reducing distractions, written reinforcement of verbal instructions, shorter focus periods with movement breaks — and reviewing them *before* medication for adults (rec. 1.5.15) [S3]. Solanto's manualized program operationalizes this with a planner/calendar as external memory, scheduling, "out of sight, out of mind" cueing to manage distracters, and self-instruction phrases [S2]. **Coach moves:** put the goal where the eyes already land; convert intentions into calendar events and visible checklists, not mental notes.

**Decompose into a tiny, concrete next action.** Both NICE-aligned structured interventions and Solanto's MCT teach "dismantling complex tasks into manageable parts" to reduce initiation friction [S2][S3]. The unit of follow-through is the smallest action that can be started now, not the outcome. **Coach move:** end every session with one next physical action specified by time and place, not a vague aspiration.

**Use if-then plans to bridge intention and action.** Implementation intentions ("If situation X arises, then I will do Y") have a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment — Gollwitzer & Sheeran's meta-analysis of 94 studies (~8,000 participants) found *d* ≈ 0.65, with larger effects when the plan uses the contingent if-then format, the person is motivated, and the plan is rehearsed [S4]. This pre-decides the moment of action, compensating for weak in-the-moment self-direction. **Coach move:** pair each goal with a trigger ("After I pour my morning coffee, I open the planner").

**Anchor goals to values and make rewards immediate.** Because distant payoffs under-motivate, Solanto's program uses contingent self-reward for completion and visualizing long-term rewards to sustain effort [S2]. SMART specificity helps mainly by forcing decomposition and a concrete target; on its own it does not solve the delayed-reward problem. **Coach move:** connect the action to a personally meaningful "why," then add a near-term reward and visual progress marker (streaks, checkmarks) so feedback arrives quickly.

**Evidence for the system as a whole.** Solanto's 12-week MCT RCT (n=88) beat an active support control on inattentive symptoms (5.0 vs 2.3-point improvement; response 42% vs 12%) [S2]. A Cochrane review found CBT-type interventions improved core ADHD symptoms vs waitlist (clinician-rated SMD ≈ −1.22; self-rated ≈ −0.84), though certainty was very-low-to-moderate and benefit over active supportive therapy was smaller [S5]. These structured, skills-based approaches are an evidence-based non-pharmacological option [S6].
