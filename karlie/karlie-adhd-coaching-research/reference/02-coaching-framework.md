### [W2.6] Barkley externalization principles for coaching — Confidence: high

Barkley reframes ADHD as a *self-regulation / executive-function deficit disorder* (EF = SR): a disorder of **performance, not knowledge** — clients fail "at the point of performance" to do what they already know [S1]. Because the impairment is in *internally represented* information, willpower, and motivation, the core treatment logic is to **move regulation out of the head and into the environment**, and to act where/when behavior actually breaks down. Coaching is therefore environmental engineering, not insight-giving [S1].

**The four operating principles (Barkley's own clinical guidance) [S1]:**

1. **Externalize information / rules.** Covert, private information is "weak as a source of stimulus control"; make it "overt and public" with physical representations placed *in the setting at the point of performance* [S1]. Coach moves: write the plan/rules on index cards laid out on the desk; sticky notes at eye level; checklists posted where the task happens; spread sub-tasks out physically so the whole problem is visible at once [S2].
2. **Externalize time.** EF deficits create "temporal myopia" — behavior is governed by the "temporal now." Make time "more externally represented," and "reduce or eliminate gaps in time among the components of a behavioral contingency (event, response, outcome)" [S1]. Coach moves: visible analog clocks, kitchen/visual countdown timers, Time Timer-style displays so elapsed time is *seen*; break long horizons into shorter, time-boxed chunks [S2][S4].
3. **Externalize motivation / incentives.** The model posits a deficit in *internally generated* motivation, so clients "require the provision of externalized sources of motivation" — artificial rewards (e.g., tokens/points) supplied *throughout* a task when natural consequences are weak or delayed. Barkley calls these "motivational prostheses," equivalent to a mechanical limb [S1]. Coach moves: build immediate, frequent, salient reward/accountability into the task (point systems, body-doubling, paired rewards), not just at completion [S1][S2].
4. **Intervene at the point of performance; reject "knowledge-only" approaches.** "Once-per-week counseling without efforts to insert accommodations at key points of performance… is unlikely to succeed" [S1]. Coaching should install supports in the *real setting and moment* of failure and accept that gains last only as long as the supports stay in place [S1].

**Why reliance on internal EF must be reduced.** Barkley's clinical rule of thumb: ADHD entails roughly a **30% developmental delay** in EF/self-regulation (he calls it "a clinical rule of thumb, not a hard and fast scientific fact"; cross-study range ~20–45%, average ~31%) — corroborated by neuroimaging showing ~3-year average cortical maturation delay (up to ~5 years prefrontal) [S3]. Practical translation: calibrate scaffolding to the client's *executive age*, not chronological age, and don't moralize willpower — supply the external system instead [S3].

### [W2.7] The ADHD coaching profession & competency frameworks — Confidence: high

**What ADHD coaching is.** ADHD coaching is a specialty *within* the broader coaching profession, not a separate clinical discipline. Practitioners are trained coaches who accept the coaching paradigm of the International Coaching Federation (ICF) and add specific training/expertise in supporting people affected by ADHD [S4]. It is a collaborative, action- and goal-oriented partnership focused on executive-function supports (planning, time, follow-through, self-regulation), distinct from therapy. The four bodies named in the brief are real and play distinct roles: **ICF** = the generalist credentialing standard; **ADDCA** = a training school; **PAAC** = an ADHD-specific credentialing body; **ACO** = a membership/professional-community association (it does not certify) [S1][S2][S3][S4].

**ICF — the underlying generalist standard [S1].** The ICF Core Competencies comprise **8 competencies grouped in 4 domains**: *Foundation* (Demonstrates Ethical Practice; Embodies a Coaching Mindset), *Co-Creating the Relationship* (Establishes & Maintains Agreements; Cultivates Trust & Safety), *Communicating Effectively* (Maintains Presence; Listens Actively), and *Cultivating Learning & Growth* (Evokes Awareness; Facilitates Client Growth) [S1]. The same competencies apply at every level; only depth/consistency changes. Credential tiers and minimums: **ACC** (60+ education hrs, 100+ coaching hrs, 10 mentor-coaching hrs), **PCC** (125+, 500+, 10), **MCC** (200+, 2,500+, 10; PCC prerequisite) [S5].

**PAAC — ADHD-specific competencies [S2][S6].** The Professional Association of ADHD Coaches frames its model around **Five Essentials**, each demonstrated through observable **Markers** (so competency is measurable): (1) *ADHD Lens* — awareness/activation of how ADHD shows up in the moment; (2) *Safety* — emotional safety/trust without shame; (3) *Collaboration* — client's choices, language, and priorities lead; (4) *Wonder* — curiosity, meaning, identity, possibility; (5) *Action* — momentum that is sustainable [S2]. PAAC credentials confirmed on its site: **CACP** (Certified ADHD Coach Practitioner; 60 ADHD-coaching hrs, ≥90% paid) and **PCAC** (Professional Certified ADHD Coach; 250 hrs, ≥90% paid); both require knowledge modules + quiz, recorded-session assessment, and 3 PAAC-approved sponsors [S6]. (Note older PAAC materials list "eleven competencies" in Partnership/Exploration/Action groupings — apparently a prior framework superseded by the current Five Essentials [S2].)

**ADDCA — the training pathway [S3].** ADD Coach Academy, founded **1998 by David Giwerc (MCC/MCAC)**, is an ADHD-specific coach-training school holding **ICF Level 1 & Level 2** and **PAAC (AACTP/CACP)** accreditation [S3]. Its Basic program is reported at ~109 training hrs (toward ICF ACC / PAAC CACP) and Advanced at ~221 hrs (toward ICF PCC / PAAC PCAC) — *reported on ADDCA marketing pages, not independently verified* [S3].

**ACO — the professional community [S4].** The ADHD Coaches Organization is a 501(c)(6) worldwide membership association ("Promoting ADHD Coaches and Coaching Worldwide"); values = community, excellence, education, advocacy. It sets ethical expectations and endorses certification through **PAAC, ICF, IAC, and CCE** but is not itself a certifying body [S4].

**Coach-facing takeaways.** "ADHD coach" is unregulated — credentials signal training, not licensure. Look for ICF (ACC/PCC/MCC) and/or PAAC (CACP/PCAC) credentials and ICF-accredited training (e.g., ADDCA). The Five Essentials [S2] are a practical session-level checklist; ICF's 8 competencies [S1] are the generalist backbone.

### [W2.8] Solanto CBT/executive-function program for adult ADHD — Confidence: high

**What it is.** Mary V. Solanto's program (Mount Sinai/Hofstra-Northwell) is a manualized, 12-session, 2-hour weekly **group** Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy that explicitly targets the *executive dysfunction* of adult ADHD — chiefly **time management, organization, and planning** — rather than mood or general symptoms [S1][S3]. Solanto frames it as "meta-cognitive" because it teaches self-management skills plus the self-directed cognitions (planning, self-monitoring, self-talk) needed to deploy them in daily life [S2]. It can be adapted for individual therapy; the full protocol is in her book *Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy for Adult ADHD: Targeting Executive Dysfunction* (Guilford, 2011) [S3]. The program won CHADD's Innovative Program of the Year Award [S3][S4].

**Structure (the 12 sessions) — a coach-usable map [S1]:**
- **S1** Making peace with the diagnosis; set personal goals; name emotions/strengths.
- **S2–S6 Time management:** constant access to clocks/watches and improving **time estimation** (S2); selecting and *actually using* a planner for to-do lists/schedules (S2); **breaking big/boring tasks into small chunks** and self-rewarding (S3); **prioritizing** ("important vs urgent") and realistic time-budgeting (S4); identifying and challenging **cognitive distortions / negative self-talk** (S5); **activation and motivation** — getting started, sustaining effort, removing sensory and social distractions (S6).
- **S7–S9 Getting organized:** set up, implement, and **maintain** an organizational system — visible/accessible storage, organizing physical spaces, handling mail/filing.
- **S10–S11 Project planning:** pick a real project, break it into steps, implement using all prior skills.
- **S12** Self-evaluate progress; plan future needs; receive a written summary of strategies.

**Signature technique — maxims/self-instructive cues** members internalize to generalize skills [S1]: *"If it's not in the planner, it doesn't exist"*; *"If you're having trouble getting started, the first step is too big"*; *"Do all things in the order of priority"*; *"A place for everything—and everything in its place"*; *"Out of sight, out of mind."* These are concrete scripts a coach can adopt directly.

**RCT evidence (verified primary source).** Solanto et al., *Am J Psychiatry* 2010;167(8):958–968 [S2]:
- **N=88** clinically-referred adults rigorously diagnosed with ADHD; stratified by ADHD-medication use, then randomized to **meta-cognitive therapy (MCT, n=45)** vs. an attention-matched **supportive psychotherapy** control (n=43) — both 12 weekly group sessions [S2].
- **Inattention symptoms** improved significantly more with MCT across **blind-clinician, self-report, and observer** ratings. On the blind clinician scale (AISRS-Inattention), MCT improved ~5.0 vs. ~2.3 points (between-group difference 2.7, 95% CI 0.9–4.6, *p*<0.005) [S2].
- **Responders: 42.2% (19/45) MCT vs. 12% (5/43) control** (χ²=10.38, *p*=.002); adjusted **odds ratio 5.41 (95% CI 1.77–16.55)** favoring MCT [S2].
- Benefit concentrated in **inattention/EF** symptoms (the program's target), not hyperactivity-impulsivity [S2]. This was the first demonstration of a non-medication treatment for adult ADHD against a control matched for therapist time/attention [S2].

**Coaching takeaway.** The session sequence is essentially an evidence-based EF-coaching curriculum: externalize time (clocks + planner), chunk tasks, prioritize, build environmental/organizational systems, then apply them to a project — paired with brief cognitive work on avoidance and negative self-talk. The maxims double as ready-made coaching cues.

2. [S2] Solanto MV, Marks DJ, Wasserstein J, et al. "Efficacy of Meta-Cognitive Therapy for Adult ADHD." *Am J Psychiatry* 2010;167(8):958–968. PMID 20231319; PMC3633586. doi:10.1176/appi.ajp.2009.09081123. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3633586/ — **Tier 2 (RCT)**

**Gaps / needs human or clinical check:**

- The 2010 RCT studied the core 12 sessions; two **optional** sessions (extra cognitive-distortion work; "Getting to bed/up/to work on time") were *not* in the trial [S1] — efficacy data do not cover them.
- Durability/long-term follow-up and effectiveness in routine (non-research) coaching settings were not established by this single trial; broader CBT-for-adult-ADHD meta-analyses would be needed to contextualize.

### [W2.9] Does ADHD coaching work? Outcome evidence — Confidence: medium

**Bottom line for the coach:** There is *real but modest* evidence that ADHD coaching helps, concentrated in college-student samples. Studies consistently show improvements in executive-functioning skills, self-regulation, and well-being — but the field has very few randomized controlled trials, most studies lack control groups and rely on self-report, and almost none show grade/GPA gains. Coaching is best framed as a *useful component of multimodal treatment*, not a standalone or proven substitute for medication/therapy [S3].

**The Edge Foundation / Wayne State study (the largest, and a true RCT).** Field, Parker, Sawilowsky & Rolands ran a randomized controlled study across 10 college campuses: 159 students with ADHD randomly assigned to Edge coaching (n=120) or a comparison group (n=39) [S1]. Coached students gained far more on the LASSI (Learning and Study Strategies Inventory, a self-report measure of academic executive functioning): mean gain of 182.67 points vs. 64.05 for comparison (p<.01) [S1]. The published paper reports a **large effect on total LASSI (d≈1.02)** and moderate-to-large effects on its Will, Skill, and Self-Regulation clusters (d≈0.65, 0.88, 1.10) [S3]. Between-group effects controlling for baseline were smaller (partial η² ≈0.04–0.08) [S3]. Coached students also reported higher well-being (p=.05) [S1][S3]. **Important caveat:** there was *no significant difference in GPA or credits earned* — the authors note the short-term model was not designed to move GPA [S1].

**Prevatt & Yelland (2015) — larger N, but no control group.** An 8-week coaching program with 148 college students (cognitive-behavioral + psychoeducational, executive-functioning framed) produced significant pre-to-post improvement across all 10 LASSI learning-strategy areas, plus gains in self-esteem, symptom distress, and satisfaction with school/work [S2]. Gains held across semesters and across coaches (all novices). But this was a *pre/post design with no comparison group*, so improvement cannot be cleanly attributed to coaching; the authors explicitly call for RCTs [S2].

**The best synthesis: Ahmann, Tuttle, Saviet & Wright (2018), descriptive review.** Reviewed 19 quantitative/qualitative coaching-outcome studies (10 specific to college students). *All 19* indicated improved ADHD symptoms and executive functioning; most quantitative studies found statistically significant benefits; 6 reported improved well-being, 3 showed maintenance of gains, 5 documented high satisfaction [S3]. **Honest limits the coach should hold:** only **2 of the 19 studies were RCTs**; **15 of 19 lacked control groups**; samples were generally small; and outcomes lean heavily on self-report (e.g., LASSI) rather than objective or blinded measures [S3]. The review's own conclusion is appropriately hedged: coaching "appears to be a useful and valued service" within multimodal treatment — not "proven efficacious" [S3].

**Coaching-relevant nuance.** Prevatt found clients with *higher initial motivation and less comorbid anxiety/depression* tended to benefit more [S3] — a reminder to set realistic expectations and coordinate with clinical care when comorbidities are significant.

**Gaps / needs human or clinical check:**
- Evidence is dominated by **college-student samples**; generalization to working adults, children, or ADHD coaching for the general adult population is weaker (adult studies exist — e.g., Kubik 2010; a 2026 prospective study reported medium-to-large effects — but were not fully verified here and many lack controls).
- No Tier 1 clinical guideline (NICE NG87, CADDRA) endorses coaching as an evidence-based treatment; that absence should be stated plainly to clients.
- A formal Cochrane-grade meta-analysis of ADHD coaching with pooled effect sizes was **not located**; the strongest synthesis remains a *descriptive* (non-meta-analytic) review.

### [W2.10] Structure of an ADHD coaching engagement — Confidence: high

ADHD coaching is "a collaborative, supportive, goal-oriented process" in which coach and client identify the client's goals and build "the self-awareness, systems, skills, and strategies" to reach them, with the coach "providing structure and accountability, as needed" [S5]. It is a wellness/skills model, **not** therapy — it "specifically targets the core impairments of ADHD such as planning, time management, goal setting, organization and problem solving" and is "not designed to help a client heal or deal with psychological barriers" (refer co-morbid anxiety/depression/regulation to clinical care) [S4]. A defensible engagement moves through six recurring elements, scaffolded by the coaching relationship.

**1. Intake / assessment.** Begin with a longer initial session — CHADD describes a "1–2 hour meeting to allow clients to reflect on their satisfaction in all areas of life" and to map "where they are now, where they want to be and how they can get there" [S4]. ADDCA frames this as the intake session plus the initial coaching agreement and goals [S2].

**2. Collaborative goal-setting & the coaching agreement.** Co-create goals; do not prescribe them. ICF Competency 3, *Establishes and Maintains Agreements* (domain B, Co-Creating the Relationship), sets agreements both for the overall engagement and for each session — including what the client wants to accomplish, how success is measured, and how the relationship will end [S1]. PAAC's *Collaboration* essential makes the same point: the coach "works alongside the client, honoring the client's preferences and direction rather than dictating outcomes" [S3].

**3. Action planning.** Translate goals into concrete, small steps. ADDCA: "By breaking large projects down into smaller, more manageable tasks, coaches keep clients more focused" [S2]. Coaching skills taught here include "strategizing, planning & goal setting, designing actions, monitoring progress, and creating accountability" [S2].

**4. Between-session accountability.** A defining ADHD-coaching feature. The coach co-designs "deadlines, schedules, meetings and regular phone check-ins," producing a level of "good stress" that keeps the brain "aroused, vigilant, and on track" [S2]. CHADD: coaches provide "encouragement, feedback and practical suggestions" while "holding them accountable for following through" [S4].

**5. Review.** Regular sessions ("30 to 60 minutes") report progress and refine step-by-step plans, with an evaluation "at the end of the contract period" [S4]. ICF Competency 8, *Facilitates Client Growth* (domain D), centers on turning learning/insight into action, designing goals with accountability, and celebrating progress [S1].

**6. Relationship / stance.** Partnership, psychological safety, and curiosity underpin everything. PAAC names *Safety* ("a judgment-free environment where clients can be vulnerable") and *Wonder* (curiosity and possibility) alongside an *ADHD Lens* that reads ADHD traits "in real time" and adjusts to the client's state [S3]; ICF parallels these in *Cultivates Trust and Safety* and *Maintains Presence* [S1].

**Evidence note:** In one evaluation of 148 college students, an 8-week coaching program produced significant improvement across all 10 learning/study-strategy areas plus self-esteem and symptom distress; authors note "additional evaluations utilizing a randomized controlled design are needed" [S6].
