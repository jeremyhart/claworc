# Fact-Checks — Distorted ADHD Statistics, Corrected

> **Why this file exists.** The Shimmer discovery corpus (and the wider ADHD internet) repeats a handful of load-bearing statistics that are distorted, mis-attributed, or stripped of their qualifiers. Each was traced back to its primary source. **Before quoting any ADHD statistic to a client, check it here.** When in doubt, give the honest range and its caveat rather than a clean viral number.

These are 11 of the 12 priority leads from the Shimmer harvest, each run back to the primary literature. (The 12th — Lally 2010's habit-formation timeline — is fully validated inside `05-habit-formation.md` §W5.19: median ~66 days, range 18–254, and 'missing one day is not failure'.)

## Verdict summary

| # | Popular claim (as it circulates) | Verdict | What's actually true |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Adult ADHD prevalence ~6% (1 in 16); 55.9% diagnosed as adults; **71.5%** had trouble filling stimulant scripts | ⚠️ Partially | 71.5% is **only among adults currently *taking* a stimulant** (~1/3 of adults w/ ADHD), during the 2022–23 shortage — not all adults with ADHD. |

## Full verdicts (primary-source detail)

### Lead: CDC MMWR vol.73 mm7340a1
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** All three numbers are reproduced correctly, but the third is missing its denominator. (1) An estimated **6.0%** of US adults had a current ADHD diagnosis (~15.5 million, "one in 16") — confirmed. (2) **55.9%** of adults with ADHD received their diagnosis in adulthood (age ≥18) — confirmed. (3) The **71.5%** figure is *not* about adults with ADHD generally, nor about a population-wide difficulty filling prescriptions. It applies only to the subset of adults **currently taking a prescription stimulant** (about one third of adults with ADHD; n≈152 in the sample): among that subset, 71.5% reported difficulty getting their ADHD prescription filled in the prior 12 months because the medication was unavailable (i.e., the 2022–2023 stimulant shortage). The blog's phrasing "71.5% reported difficulty filling stimulant prescriptions" drops the critical qualifier that this is among stimulant *users*, which inflates the apparent scope.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** Among the ~1 in 3 adults with ADHD who took a prescription stimulant in the past 12 months, **71.5% reported difficulty filling that prescription because the medication was unavailable** — not 71.5% of all adults with ADHD.
- **Source quality:** Cross-sectional national probability survey (commercial-panel-based online/mail survey). National Center for Health Statistics Rapid Surveys System (RSS), Round 2, fielded October–November 2023. Total n = 7,046 completed adult interviews; 444 adults with current ADHD; stimulant-medication subset n≈152 (the basis for the 71.5% estimate, which therefore has a wide confidence interval). Self-reported diagnosis and medication use; not clinically verified.
- **Real citation:** Staley BS, Robinson LR, Claussen AH, Katz SM, Danielson ML, Summers AD, Farr SL, Blumberg SJ, Tinker SC. "Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder Diagnosis, Treatment, and Telehealth Use in Adults — National Center for Health Statistics Rapid Surveys System, United States, October–November 2023." *MMWR Morb Mortal Wkly Rep* 2024;73(40):890–895. DOI: 10.15585/mmwr.mm7340a1. https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/73/wr/mm7340a1.htm (PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11466376/)

### Lead: PMC5567978
- **Verdict:** supports
- **What the source actually says:** The article explicitly states that "emotional dysregulation is a distinctive attribute of adult ADHD psychopathology, however these symptoms may be misdiagnosed as a mood disorder" — matching the blog's core claim. On the numbers: it reports that "adults with ADHD were 42% less likely to be employed full-time as were adults without ADHD (rates of full-time employment: 34% vs 59%, respectively, *P* < 0.001)," and that "one study estimated the individual income reduction in adults with ADHD in the United States to be between $8900 and $15,400 annually." Both employment/income figures are not original to this paper — they are cited to Biederman & Faraone (2006), *MedGenMed*.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** Largely n/a. One nuance: the $15,400 is the *upper bound* of a range ($8,900–$15,400 annual individual income reduction), not a single point estimate. The 42% figure is a *relative reduction* in likelihood of full-time employment (34% vs 59% absolute rates), not a 42-percentage-point gap.
- **Source quality:** Narrative review article in *BMC Psychiatry* (peer-reviewed, open access, CC BY 4.0); no primary sample of its own. The employment/income statistics it relays originate from a separate primary source (Biederman & Faraone 2006, an analysis of U.S. survey data).
- **Real citation:** Katzman MA, Bilkey TS, Chokka PR, Fallu A, Klassen LJ. "Adult ADHD and comorbid disorders: clinical implications of a dimensional approach." *BMC Psychiatry*. 2017;17(1):302. doi:10.1186/s12888-017-1463-3 — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5567978/

### Lead: SAGE 10.1177/1087054718816164
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** The study estimated, via a life-expectancy calculator fed 14 risk variables (not observed deaths), that childhood hyperactivity/ADHD-Combined type was associated with a **9.5-year reduction in *healthy* estimated life expectancy (ELE)** and an **8.4-year reduction in *total* ELE** versus controls by adulthood. Where ADHD **persisted into adulthood**, the reduction in ELE rose to **12.7 years**. So the real headline figures are ~8.4–12.7 years (with 9.5 for healthy ELE), driven by modifiable factors. The blog's "Barkley & Fischer" attribution and the general claim that ADHD is associated with substantially reduced *estimated* life expectancy are correct, but its specific range "6.5 to 11 years" does not appear in this paper.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** ADHD was associated with an estimated reduction of **8.4 years (total ELE) to 9.5 years (healthy ELE)** for childhood ADHD vs. controls, rising to **12.7 years when ADHD persisted into adulthood** — not "6.5 to 11 years." Critically, these are *modeled/estimated* life-expectancy figures derived from a calculator and behavioral risk factors, not measured/observed deaths in the cohort.
- **Source quality:** Prospective case-control follow-up study (Milwaukee longitudinal cohort) in the *Journal of Attention Disorders* (2019; Vol. 23, No. 9, pp. 907–923), a peer-reviewed SAGE journal. Sample: **131 hyperactive cases and 71 controls** at young-adult follow-up. Estimates come from a published life-expectancy calculator applied to 14 derived variables, so they are projections, not death-rate observations.
- **Real citation:** Barkley RA, Fischer M. "Hyperactive Child Syndrome and Estimated Life Expectancy at Young Adult Follow-Up: The Role of ADHD Persistence and Other Potential Predictors." *Journal of Attention Disorders.* 2019;23(9):907–923. doi:10.1177/1087054718816164. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30526189/ • https://doi.org/10.1177/1087054718816164

### Lead: PubMed 25726514
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** This Danish nationwide cohort study (1.92 million people, 32,061 with ADHD, 24.9 million person-years, 5,580 deaths) found that people with ADHD had a fully adjusted **mortality rate ratio of 2.07** (95% CI 1.70–2.50; p<0.0001) — roughly **double** the all-cause mortality of people without ADHD (5.85 vs 2.21 deaths per 10,000 person-years). The excess deaths were driven overwhelmingly by **unnatural causes, especially accidents** — not suicide. The relative risk rose sharply with age at diagnosis (MRR 4.25 for those diagnosed at 18+) and, after excluding comorbid oppositional defiant/conduct/substance-use disorders, ADHD alone still carried an MRR of 1.50. So the "increased mortality" half of the claim is well-supported; the "suicide risk" framing is not what this paper emphasizes — accidents, not suicide, were the leading cause.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** ADHD was associated with **~2x higher all-cause mortality** (fully adjusted MRR 2.07, 95% CI 1.70–2.50). The increased mortality was **primarily from accidents (unnatural causes), not suicide**; this study does not report a suicide-specific risk ratio as its headline finding. If the blog cited this paper specifically for "suicide risk," that attribution is inaccurate — the appropriate framing is elevated accidental/all-cause mortality.
- **Source quality:** Nationwide register-based prospective cohort study; *The Lancet* (a top-tier general medical journal, very high quality); very large sample (1,920,000 individuals; 32,061 with ADHD; 5,580 deaths over 24.9 million person-years).
- **Real citation:** Dalsgaard S, Østergaard SD, Leckman JF, Mortensen PB, Pedersen MG. "Mortality in children, adolescents, and adults with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder: a nationwide cohort study." *The Lancet*, 2015;385(9983):2190–2196. DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(14)61684-6 — https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25726514/

### Lead: PMC10498662
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** PMC10498662 is a mixed-method systematic review of *undiagnosed* ADHD and/or autism (not "untreated"). It synthesizes 17 studies (14 on ADHD, 3 on ASD) and concludes that "undiagnosed ASD/ADHD is linked to many risks and negative outcomes affecting individuals, their families, and the wider society." Suicide-related harm is named as one such risk: it cites that adults with undiagnosed ADHD in substance-use populations had "higher rates of suicide attempts and depression" (Huntley et al., 2012) and that undiagnosed psychiatric inpatients "were more likely to die by suicide" (Bitter et al., 2019). It also notes that ADHD drug treatment "may reduce suicide rates" per Swedish data. Crucially, the review reports **no hazard ratio, odds ratio, relative risk, or mortality figure of its own** — it does not quantify suicide or all-cause mortality risk, and the cited findings are qualitative ("higher rates," "more likely") drawn from the included studies.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** The blog's "elevated mortality and suicide risk" framing should be qualified: (a) this source addresses **undiagnosed** ADHD, not "untreated" ADHD; (b) it provides **no quantitative mortality or suicide figure** — only a qualitative association with elevated suicide-attempt rates and a higher likelihood of suicide death in specific subpopulations (substance-use patients, psychiatric inpatients). For an actual all-cause mortality estimate, a different primary source (e.g., the Dalsgaard et al. 2015 *Lancet* Danish cohort, adjusted mortality rate ratio ≈ 2.07) would be needed; this paper does not contain one.
- **Source quality:** Mixed-method systematic review (qualitative synthesis, no meta-analytic effect sizes); *Journal of Attention Disorders*; 17 included studies (14 ADHD, 3 ASD).
- **Real citation:** French B, Daley D, Groom M, Cassidy S. "Risks Associated With Undiagnosed ADHD and/or Autism: A Mixed-Method Systematic Review." *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 2023. DOI: 10.1177/10870547231176862 — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10498662/

### Lead: AJP 10.1176/appi.ajp.2013.13070966
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** This is a *narrative/conceptual review* of the literature, not an original study, and it does not report a single "70% of adults are impatient/quick to anger" statistic. Its actual claim is that **emotion dysregulation is present in roughly 34%–70% of adults with ADHD** — a *range*, with 70% as the upper bound, not a point estimate. The figure refers to **emotion dysregulation broadly** (excessive, rapidly shifting emotions; irritability; low frustration tolerance; aggression), not specifically to "impatience / quick to anger." Those particular descriptors derive from Barkley's concept of "deficient emotional self-regulation / emotional impulsiveness," which the review discusses and cites rather than originates. The review's three headline findings are that emotion dysregulation is prevalent across the lifespan and drives impairment, that it implicates a striato-amygdalo-medial prefrontal network, and that it reframes treatment questions. (The comparable child figure the review gives is ~24%–50%.)
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** "Per a 2014 American Journal of Psychiatry review, emotion dysregulation — excessive, rapidly shifting emotions including irritability, low frustration tolerance, and quickness to anger — affects an estimated **34% to 70% of adults with ADHD**." Do not present 70% as a precise prevalence of "impatience/anger"; it is the top of a wide range for emotion dysregulation overall, and the underlying numbers come from primary studies the review summarizes (e.g., Barkley), not from new data in this paper.
- **Source quality:** Narrative/conceptual review (combining qualitative and quantitative synthesis of prior literature); *American Journal of Psychiatry* (high-impact, peer-reviewed). No original sample — it aggregates other studies, so it is a secondary source, not primary epidemiological evidence.
- **Real citation:** Shaw P, Stringaris A, Nigg J, Leibenluft E. "Emotion Dysregulation in Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder." *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2014;171(3):276–293. DOI: 10.1176/appi.ajp.2013.13070966. PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24480998/ (Note: this DOI is a review article, so it is itself a *secondary* citation; a blog using it as the origin of the 34–70% figure is citing a review, not the primary data.)

### Lead: Springer 10.1007/s11920-017-0860-0
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** This is a narrative literature review, not an original prevalence study. The abstract states: *"Three cross-sectional, clinical, and population studies report a prevalence of insomnia in ADHD adults ranging from 43 to 80%."* So 80% is the **upper bound of a 43–80% range** drawn from just three studies, and it refers specifically to **insomnia** (clinically defined as dissatisfaction with sleep quantity/quality), not "sleep problems" broadly. For comparison, the review notes insomnia prevalence in the general population is already 31–56%, so the ADHD figure is elevated but the high end comes from one tail of a wide range.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** The blog's "up to 80%" is technically traceable to this source's upper bound, but it is misleading on two counts: (1) the source reports a **range of 43–80%**, with 80% being the maximum across only three studies, not a typical or consensus value; and (2) the source measures **insomnia specifically**, not the broader category of "sleep problems." A faithful restatement: *"A 2017 literature review found that three studies reported insomnia in 43–80% of adults with ADHD."*
- **Source quality:** Narrative/literature review (publication type: Review), *Current Psychiatry Reports*, 2017. No original sample — it summarizes prior studies; the 43–80% figure itself derives from only three cross-sectional/clinical/population studies, whose individual sample sizes are not given in the abstract.
- **Real citation:** Wynchank D, Bijlenga D, Beekman AT, Kooij JJS, Penninx BW. *Adult Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) and Insomnia: an Update of the Literature.* Current Psychiatry Reports. 2017;19(12):98. DOI: 10.1007/s11920-017-0860-0 — https://doi.org/10.1007/s11920-017-0860-0 (PMID: 29086065, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29086065/)

### Lead: PMC8187558
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** In a sample of 252 adult ADHD patients, **44.4%** met criteria for **insomnia disorder** (this matches the blog's "44%"). The study also reports that **63.9%** of the whole sample "had insomnia symptoms" (≈64%, so the blog's "64% experience insomnia symptoms" is essentially correct as to magnitude). However, the figure the blog appears to be citing as 64% — **64.3%** — is NOT a whole-sample symptom rate; in the paper, 64.3% is the prevalence of insomnia *disorder* specifically *within the combined-presentation ADHD subtype only* ("more common in combined presentation (64.3%)"). The two 64-ish numbers are a coincidence: the blog's "64% have insomnia symptoms" claim corresponds to the real 63.9% symptom figure, but if the blog pulled "64%" from the 64.3% number, it mislabeled a subtype-specific disorder rate as a population-wide symptom rate.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** Both claims are defensible if read as: "44.4% of adult ADHD patients met criteria for insomnia disorder; 63.9% reported insomnia symptoms." Caution: 64.3% is not a general "symptoms" figure — it is insomnia-disorder prevalence in the combined-presentation subgroup. The accurate whole-sample symptom figure is 63.9%, and these come from a single clinical cross-sectional study (not a population epidemiological estimate), so the figures should not be generalized to all adults with ADHD.
- **Source quality:** Cross-sectional observational study at an adult ADHD outpatient clinic; n = 252 adult ADHD patients (mean age 37.6 ± 13.2 yrs); DSM-5 diagnoses; validated instruments (Insomnia Severity Index, Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index, Epworth Sleepiness Scale). Published in *Frontiers in Psychiatry* (peer-reviewed, open access). Limitations: single-center clinical sample, cross-sectional design (no causal inference), not a representative population sample.
- **Real citation:** Fadeuilhe C, Daigre C, Richarte V, Grau-López L, Palma-Álvarez RF, Corrales M, Ramos-Quiroga JA. "Insomnia Disorder in Adult Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder Patients: Clinical, Comorbidity, and Treatment Correlates." *Frontiers in Psychiatry*. 2021 May 26;12:663889. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2021.663889. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8187558/

### Lead: ScienceDirect S0165032721005127
- **Verdict:** partially supports
- **What the source actually says:** This 2021 systematic review + meta-analysis found that mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs) outperformed *waiting-list / inactive controls* on ADHD symptoms in both adults and children — a medium pooled effect size in adults, and a "very large" reduction in inattention in the inactive-controlled subset (5 studies, 261 participants). But the authors' central finding is the opposite of a clean win: in *active-controlled* studies (vs psychoeducation/skills training), the effect dropped to "low/negligible" and was statistically non-significant. For overall ADHD symptoms, the 4 active-controlled studies (302 participants) showed no significant difference; for inattention, the 5 active-controlled studies (335 participants) showed no significant difference. A publication bias was detected for several outcomes, and after adjustment the hyperactivity effect became non-significant. The authors conclude MBIs "may be useful as complementation and not as replacement of other active interventions" and that they could not conclude MBIs are superior to other active interventions. (No children's studies used active controls at all.)
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** The unqualified claim "mindfulness improves ADHD symptoms" overstates the source. A faithful statement: "A 2021 meta-analysis (31 studies) found mindfulness-based interventions reduced ADHD symptoms versus waitlist/no-treatment controls (medium effect in adults), but showed no significant benefit over active psychological treatments such as psychoeducation and skills training — and the authors recommend MBIs only as a complement, not a replacement, for established treatments. Publication bias affected several results."
- **Source quality:** Systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression (PRISMA-compliant); *Journal of Affective Disorders*, vol. 292 (2021), pp. 475–486; 31 full-text studies included (mix of controlled and observational, adults and children); per-outcome meta-analytic pools ranged ~4–10 studies and ~243–596 participants (e.g., overall ADHD symptoms: 7 studies/489 participants; inattention: 10 studies/596 participants). Authors note further studies with better methodology are needed.
- **Real citation:** Oliva F, Malandrone F, di Girolamo G, Mirabella S, Colombi N, Carletto S, Ostacoli L. "The efficacy of mindfulness-based interventions in attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder beyond core symptoms: A systematic review, meta-analysis, and meta-regression." *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 2021;292:475–486. DOI: [10.1016/j.jad.2021.05.068](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2021.05.068) · PMID: [34146899](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34146899/)

*Access note: the ScienceDirect page itself returned HTTP 403, but the identifier was verified via DOI resolution (10.1016/j.jad.2021.05.068 → PII S0165032721005127), the PubMed abstract (PMID 34146899), and the open-access author manuscript at the University of Turin repository (iris.unito.it/handle/2318/1797152).*

### Lead: Nature s41588-022-01171-3
- **Verdict:** supports
- **What the source actually says:** The cited paper's Introduction states verbatim: *"ADHD and ASD are both highly heritable (60–93%)... and the mode of their inheritance is complex and polygenic."* This 60–93% range is a **twin/family-based heritability** figure, presented as background and attributed to prior studies (references 2–4), not derived from this paper's own analysis. The paper's *own* original contribution is a **SNP-based heritability** of 0.128 (12.8%) on the liability scale for the combined ADHD/ASD phenotype — a much lower number that the paper explicitly contrasts against the high family-based estimates ("Despite high family-based heritability estimates, GWAS have only recently identified common variants..."). The blog's "60–93%" matches the quoted twin-heritability range exactly.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** n/a — the 60–93% range matches the source verbatim. One caveat worth noting: 60–93% is twin/family heritability borrowed from earlier studies, not a finding original to this paper; and it should not be conflated with the paper's own SNP-heritability estimate of ~12.8%.
- **Source quality:** Genome-wide association study (GWAS) with cross-disorder analyses, published in *Nature Genetics* (a top-tier peer-reviewed journal). Sample: 34,462 ADHD and/or ASD cases and 41,201 controls (Psychiatric Genomics Consortium + iPSYCH/Lundbeck cohorts). Note: the paper is a primary GWAS, so for the 60–93% claim specifically it is acting as a *secondary* source citing earlier twin studies (refs 2–4).
- **Real citation:** Mattheisen M, Grove J, Als TD, Martin J, Voloudakis G, Meier S, ... Børglum AD. "Identification of shared and differentiating genetic architecture for autism spectrum disorder, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder and case subgroups." *Nature Genetics*. 2022;54(10):1470–1478. ### Lead: Dodson 20,000 statements
- **Verdict:** does not support (the figure is real and widely circulated, but it is a hypothetical arithmetic estimate, not a research finding; the blog's specifics — attribution, age, and "more than peers" — are wrong)
- **What the source actually says:** The "20,000" number originates not with William Dodson but with child psychiatrist **Michael S. Jellinek, MD**, in a 2010 opinion/commentary column. He presents it as an explicit back-of-the-envelope thought experiment, not a measurement: he asks the reader to *assume* a child with ADHD gets a corrective comment from the teacher about "three times an hour," then multiplies — "6 hours a day for 180 days of school each year. That's more than 3,200 nonpositive comments directed at a child each year." He concludes: *"In school alone, a child with ADHD could receive 20,000 corrective or negative comments by the time he or she is age 10."* Critically: (1) it is **age 10**, not 12; (2) it is the child's **total** count of corrective comments from teachers "in school alone," **not** a "20,000 *more than* non-ADHD peers" differential; (3) the word he uses is "could," and the whole calculation is hypothetical ("It's not too much of a stretch to assume... Let's say..."). No survey, observational data, or study underlies the number — it is illustrative arithmetic. Dodson is widely quoted (e.g., via CHADD) with a paraphrased variant — "By their twelfth birthday, children with ADHD receive around 20,000 negative messages" — but that, too, is attributed to him with **no peer-reviewed citation**, and it appears to be a drift of Jellinek's original illustration.
- **Corrected figure (if the blog version was off):** A child with ADHD "could receive 20,000 corrective or negative comments **by age 10**, **in school alone**" — a hypothetical estimate (≈3 corrections/hour × 6 hours × 180 days ≈ 3,200/year) attributable to **Michael S. Jellinek, MD**, *not* to a study and *not* framed as "20,000 *more than* peers by age 12." The honest framing is: "It has been estimated (illustratively, not measured) that a child with ADHD could accumulate on the order of 20,000 corrective comments at school by around age 10."
- **Source quality:** Not a study. It is a single-author **opinion/commentary column** ("COMMENTARY") in *Clinical Psychiatry News* (a non-peer-reviewed trade/news publication, now MDedge), containing zero data collection — no sample, no methods, no measurement. The number is the author's own arithmetic hypothetical. The Dodson-attributed version (CHADD, ADDitude) is likewise an uncited clinical assertion. (A genuinely peer-reviewed ADHD study sometimes cited nearby — Furukawa et al., *J. Child Psychol. Psychiatry*, 2016, n=210, on punishment/failure avoidance — is a different study and is **not** the source of the 20,000 figure.)
- **Real citation:** Jellinek, Michael S. "Don't Let ADHD Crush Children's Self-Esteem" (Commentary). *Clinical Psychiatry News*, May 2010, p. 12. https://www.mdedge.com/psychiatry/article/23971/pediatrics/dont-let-adhd-crush-childrens-self-esteem (original article PDF: https://cdn.mdedge.com/files/s3fs-public/issues/articles/70231_main_7.pdf). Dodson-attributed variant, with no study citation: CHADD, "Prioritize Praising Your Child with ADHD," https://chadd.org/prioritize-praising-your-child-with-adhd/.
