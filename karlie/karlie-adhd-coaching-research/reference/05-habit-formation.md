### [W5.19] Science of habit formation (myth-busting) — Confidence: high

**The 21-day "rule" is a myth — and here's where it came from.** The "21 days to form a habit" claim has no basis in habit research. It originated from plastic surgeon Maxwell Maltz's 1960 self-help book *Psycho-Cybernetics*, where he observed that patients took "a minimum of about 21 days" to emotionally *adjust* to a new face or amputation — an observation about self-image, not habit acquisition. Self-help authors stripped the context and rebranded it as a universal habit law [S1][S3]. **Coaching takeaway:** when a client says "I've been at this 3 weeks and it's not automatic, what's wrong with me?" — nothing is wrong. The premise was never real.

**What the actual data says (Lally et al., 2010).** In the most-cited real-world study, 96 volunteers picked one daily eating, drinking, or activity behaviour and performed it in a consistent context for 12 weeks (84 days), rating automaticity daily. Researchers fit an asymptotic curve to each person. The **median time to reach automaticity was 66 days**, but the individual range ran from **18 to 254 days** — roughly a 14-fold spread [S2]. **Coaching takeaways:**
- Use ~66 days as a *median*, never a deadline. Set client expectations toward "a couple of months and highly variable," not a fixed number.
- The huge range matters more than the median: a client who automates a behaviour in 3 weeks and one who needs 8 months are *both* normal. Individual differences (and behaviour complexity) dominate. Wendy Wood similarly states it "can take two to three months to form a simple habit" [S4].
- Habit growth is a *curve that plateaus*, not a switch that flips — early reps yield big automaticity gains, then returns diminish. Frame progress as a rising-then-leveling line.

**Missing a day is not failure.** Lally found that **missing a single opportunity did not materially impair habit formation**; consistency over the long run mattered more than perfection [S2]. **Coaching takeaway:** kill the "I broke the chain, might as well quit" spiral. One miss costs almost nothing; abandoning the routine costs everything. This is especially protective for ADHD clients prone to all-or-nothing thinking.

**The mechanism: context–repetition–reward → automaticity (Wood).** A habit is "a mental association between a context cue and a response," built by repeating an action in a stable context for a reward [S4][S5]. Three levers:
- **Context cue** — same time, place, or preceding event triggers the behaviour. Stable environments breed habits; changed environments break them (useful both ways) [S4].
- **Repetition** — each rep in the same context strengthens the cue→response link and lowers the mental effort required [S4][S5].
- **Reward, and it must be *immediate*** — Wood notes "dopamine works for maybe up to a minute," so delayed rewards barely reinforce. Engineer an instant, intrinsic, or tangible payoff [S4]. (ADHD-relevant: immediacy of reward is a known weak point.)

Wood's reframe for coaches: high self-control people don't out-willpower others — they "choose situations in which it's easier to repeat desired actions." Design the environment, don't rely on motivation [S4].

**Cue–routine–reward "habit loop" (Duhigg/Graybiel).** Duhigg's popular *cue → routine → reward* loop (*The Power of Habit*, 2012) is the same structure in plainer language, grounded in Ann Graybiel's MIT basal-ganglia research: as a behaviour becomes habitual, neural control shifts from deliberative prefrontal regions toward the striatum, and the encoded loop persists even after long disuse [S3]. **Coaching takeaway:** to change a "bad" habit, keep the cue and reward but swap the routine, rather than fighting the cue.

### [W5.20] Implementation intentions (if-then planning) — Confidence: high

**What they are (vs. goal intentions).** A *goal intention* specifies what you want to achieve ("I will exercise more"). An *implementation intention* is a subordinate plan that pre-specifies the *when, where, and how*, in an explicit if-then format: **"If situation Y occurs, then I will initiate goal-directed response Z!"** [S1][S2]. The plan ties a concrete situational cue to a concrete action in advance, so the cue itself later triggers the behavior.

**Why they work (mechanisms).** Forming an if-then plan does two things [S1]: (1) it heightens the *accessibility* of the specified cue — the mental representation of the anticipated situation becomes highly activated, so the person is primed to notice the critical moment when it arrives; and (2) it builds *strategic automaticity* — a strong cue→response association so that action initiation becomes "automated... immediacy, efficiency, and redundancy of conscious intent" [S1]. In plain terms: the situation pulls the behavior out of you, instead of you having to remember, decide, and self-start in the moment.

**The evidence (the headline statistic, verified).** Gollwitzer & Sheeran's (2006) meta-analysis of **94 independent tests (n = 8,461)** found implementation intentions had a **medium-to-large effect on goal attainment, Cohen's d = 0.65 (95% CI ≈ 0.60–0.70)** [S2][S3][S4]. Beyond *starting* goals, the review found they also help *shield* ongoing goal pursuit from distraction, *disengage* from failing actions, and *conserve* self-regulatory capacity [S3]. (Note: this is a broad meta-analysis across health, achievement, and relationship goals in general populations, not an ADHD-specific effect.)

**Why this is especially relevant to ADHD.** ADHD involves executive-function and self-activation deficits — the classic "intention–action gap," where knowing the goal does not reliably produce the action [S5]. If-then plans externalize the trigger and offload the act of initiation from effortful prefrontal control to a near-automatic cue response — exactly the function ADHD brains struggle to supply on demand [S5]. Direct evidence: in children with ADHD, furnishing a suppression goal with an implementation intention improved Go/No-Go response inhibition **to the level of non-ADHD peers**, and combining if-then plans with stimulant medication produced the highest performance of all [S6]. For *adult* ADHD, an open trial of CBT (no medication) showed significant gains on an activation measure, but Ramsay cautions the strategy "hasn't been extensively studied" in adults [S5].

**Coaching how-to (practical).**
- Convert vague goals into a specific cue + specific action. Not "I'll deal with email"; rather *"If it's 9:00 a.m. and I sit down, then I open only my inbox and clear three messages."* [S5]
- Make the *cue* something that will actually occur and be noticed (a time, a location, the end of a meeting, finishing coffee) — cue specificity is what drives the effect [S1].
- Build plans for predictable failure points, not just the start: re-engagement after a distraction/break, and counters to rationalizations ("If I tell myself 'I'll do it later,' then I set a 5-minute timer and begin") [S5].
- Externalize plans as portable, "sticky" coping cards / reminders so the client doesn't have to hold them in working memory [S5].

### [W5.21] Fogg Behavior Model / Tiny Habits — Confidence: medium

**The model (framework).** BJ Fogg (Stanford Behavior Design Lab) frames behavior as **B = MAP**: a Behavior happens only when **M**otivation, **A**bility, and a **P**rompt converge at the same moment; "when a behavior does not occur, at least one of those three elements is missing" [S1]. Practically, if a client isn't doing something, diagnose *which* element failed rather than assuming a willpower deficit: was the prompt absent (no trigger), was ability too low (too hard, too slow, too costly), or was motivation too low? Fogg's central design move is to **raise ability (shrink the behavior) rather than chase motivation**, because motivation is unreliable and fluctuates [S1][S4]. This is highly relevant to ADHD, where motivation and "discipline" are inconsistent and prompt-failure (forgetting/inattention) is common — the model lets a coach redesign the *behavior and its trigger* instead of exhorting effort.

**Tiny Habits method (framework).** Fogg's applied recipe has three parts: an **Anchor** (an existing routine that already happens reliably), the **Tiny Behavior** (a scaled-down version of the target habit, small enough to do "even on your worst day"), and **immediate Celebration** (a genuine positive feeling right after) [S2][S4]. The format is essentially *"After I [anchor], I will [tiny behavior]"* — anchoring solves the ADHD prompt problem by borrowing reliability from a behavior that's already automatic.

**What's actually evidence-tested vs. asserted.**
- **Anchoring = implementation intentions (well-tested mechanism).** The "After I X, I will Y" structure is operationally an **implementation intention** (if-then plan), an independent research line by Gollwitzer. Its 2006 meta-analysis (94 independent tests) reported a **medium-to-large effect, d ≈ 0.65**, on goal attainment over goal-intentions alone, working by increasing cue accessibility and automating the cue→response link [S3]. So the *anchoring* component rests on robust general evidence.
- **In ADHD specifically:** if-then plans have **direct experimental support in children with ADHD** — Gawrilow & Gollwitzer (2008) showed implementation intentions improved Go/No-Go response inhibition (and normalized P300 markers), adding to medication effects [S5]. This is the strongest ADHD-relevant signal, but it targets *inhibition in lab tasks*, not real-world habit formation, and is in **children**; effects in **adults with ADHD have not been directly trialed** [S5]. If-then planning is also embedded in CBT for adult ADHD (e.g., Ramsay-style "turning intentions into actions"), but as part of a package, not isolated.
- **Celebration / "emotions create habits" (framework, not RCT-tested).** Fogg's claim that immediate positive emotion is what wires the habit is a plausible reinforcement-learning rationale but is **his framework, not validated by controlled habit-formation trials** [S2][S4]. Treat as a useful, low-risk coaching practice, not an evidence claim.
- **Fogg Behavior Model overall:** a 2025 scoping review found only **6 intervention studies (4 RCTs, 2 non-randomized; n=289 total)** applying the FBM, with positive results in domains like vaccination, diabetes self-management, and prenatal care — but small samples, no long-term follow-up, no head-to-head vs. other models, and the authors call it "underutilized" [S6]. **No ADHD studies appear in that review** [S6].

**Coach guidance.** Use B=MAP as a diagnostic ("which of M/A/P broke?"), default to *shrinking the behavior* over boosting motivation, and always **attach the new behavior to a concrete existing anchor** — that anchoring step is the part with the strongest evidence for ADHD. Frame celebration as worth trying, not as proven.

### [W5.22] ADHD-specific habit scaffolding — Confidence: high

**Why standard habit advice underperforms for ADHD.** Mainstream habit advice ("just be consistent," "use willpower," "it takes 21/66 days") assumes intact executive function (EF). Barkley reframes ADHD as a self-regulation/EF deficit disorder: a "disorder mainly of performance rather than of knowledge or skills" — clients usually *know* what to do but fail "at the point of performance," the specific place and time the behavior must happen [S1]. Two mechanisms make repetition-based advice fail. (1) **Temporal myopia:** behavior is "governed even more than normal by events close to or within the temporal now," so distant payoffs (health, a finished project) exert little pull [S1]. This is empirically grounded: meta-analysis finds people with ADHD reliably prefer small immediate over larger delayed rewards (37 group comparisons, 3,763 participants), and the World Federation consensus rates this a "moderate" effect [S2][S3]. (2) **Weak internal motivation + working-memory load:** intentions held "in the head" are, in Barkley's words, "extraordinarily weak in their ability to control and sustain behavior"; internal motivation must be "augmented with more powerful external forms" [S1]. Motivation also skews to interest/novelty/urgency rather than importance (an "interest-based" pattern, clinically described by Dodson but expert opinion, not trial-tested) [S4].

**What actually works — coach-facing tactics.**
- **Externalize everything at the point of performance.** "Covert or private information is weak as a source of stimulus control; making that information overt and public" strengthens it [S1]. Put cues, checklists, and visible timers *where and when* the action happens — not on a planner reviewed elsewhere. NICE NG87 formally endorses **environmental modifications** (physical changes: reduced noise, written reminders, movement breaks) and, for adults, has clients implement these *before* escalating care [S5].
- **Make time and rewards immediate and visible.** Shrink temporal gaps between event→response→reward; "do a step a day" with "immediate feedback and incentives" rather than month-long contingencies [S1]. Build in **artificial/immediate rewards** — Barkley likens these "motivational prostheses" to "mechanical limbs… for the physically disabled" [S1]. In the reward meta-analysis, offering *real* rewards "almost doubled the odds ratio," confirming immediacy and tangibility matter [S2]. Gamification works by the same logic; treat points/streaks as scaffolds, not character tests.
- **Habit stacking / if-then plans.** Anchor a new habit to an existing cue ("after I pour coffee, I take meds"). This is the popularized form of **implementation intentions**, which have strong general RCT support (Gollwitzer); an ADHD-specific RCT found if-then plans improved delay-of-gratification performance in children with ADHD to levels comparable to peers [S6].
- **Novelty rotation:** because motivation is interest/novelty-driven, periodically refresh the form of a habit (new playlist, location, tool) to re-recruit engagement [S4] — practice-based, not trial-verified.
- **Body doubling:** working alongside another person to borrow activation/accountability. Widely used and plausibly effective via social facilitation, but evidence is thin and mixed — small experimental studies, anecdote-driven, and some neurodivergent people find social presence aversive [S7][S8]. Offer it; don't promise it.
