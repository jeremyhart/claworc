### [W1.1] ADHD neurobiology & executive-function model — Confidence: high

**Core reframe a coach must internalize: ADHD is a self-regulation / executive-function problem, not a deficit of attention or willpower.** Both major theorists converge here. Russell Barkley argues the *central* deficit is impaired **behavioral (response) inhibition** — the capacity to pause before acting and protect a delay — which is foundational to four executive functions: (1) nonverbal working memory, (2) self-regulation of affect/motivation/arousal, (3) internalization of speech (self-talk/self-direction), and (4) reconstitution (planning, problem-solving) [S1]. Thomas Brown frames ADHD as impairment in the brain's "management system," describing **six interacting clusters**: **Activation** (organizing, prioritizing, initiating — chronic procrastination until a task becomes an emergency), **Focus** (directing, sustaining, shifting attention; includes hyperfocus/"locking on"), **Effort** (regulating alertness, sustaining effort, processing speed, sleep), **Emotion** (low frustration threshold; emotions "flood" and crowd out other thoughts), **Memory** (working memory and retrieval, not long-term storage), and **Action** (monitoring and self-regulating behavior and social context) [S2]. Brown stresses these are *inherited differences in brain chemistry*, evidenced by the hallmark paradox: people can focus intensely on high-interest activities yet stall on important-but-dull tasks — so it is not "lacking willpower" [S2].

**Coaching implication:** the limiting factor for most clients is *getting started, sustaining effort, holding goals in mind, and regulating emotion in the moment* — not knowing what to do. Externalize executive functions (visible timers, written cues, body-doubling, breaking tasks down, immediate stakes) rather than exhorting effort [S1][S2].

**The brain systems.** Self-regulation depends on the **prefrontal cortex (PFC)**, which exerts "top-down" control: directing attention to goal-relevant (often non-salient) material, inhibiting inappropriate responses, and regulating emotion [S3]. The PFC requires an **optimal level of dopamine (DA) and norepinephrine (NE)** to function — too little *or* too much impairs it, an **inverted-U** relationship. NE acting on **α2A receptors** strengthens relevant network connections; DA acting on **D1 receptors** reduces "noise" by dampening irrelevant inputs [S3]. Large-scale NIMH/NIH imaging (10,000+ scans, 8,000+ youth) found ADHD symptoms tied to atypical connectivity between frontal control regions and deep structures handling reward, movement, learning, and emotion (caudate, putamen, nucleus accumbens) [S4]. The World Federation consensus documents slightly reduced cortical surface area and smaller subcortical/frontal-cingulate-temporal regions at the group level [S5].

**Two cautions for credibility.** (1) **Brain differences are group averages and cannot diagnose any individual** — never imply a scan "shows" a client's ADHD [S4][S5]. (2) ADHD is **highly heritable** (mean ~74% across twin studies), so it is a neurodevelopmental difference, not poor parenting or laziness — useful for de-shaming clients [S6]. Note all first-line medications work by **enhancing catecholamine (DA/NE) signaling in the PFC** (stimulants increase DA/NE release; atomoxetine blocks NE reuptake; guanfacine mimics NE at α2A) [S3] — relevant context, though prescribing is outside a coach's scope.

### [W1.2] DSM-5-TR criteria & presentations across demographics — Confidence: high

**Core diagnostic criteria (DSM-5-TR; criteria unchanged from DSM-5, which the AAFP table reproduces) [S1][S2]**
ADHD is diagnosed from two symptom domains of 9 items each — **inattention** (e.g., careless mistakes, poor sustained attention, doesn't seem to listen, fails to finish tasks, poor organization, avoids sustained-effort tasks, loses things, easily distracted, forgetful) and **hyperactivity-impulsivity** (e.g., fidgets, leaves seat, restlessness/"driven by a motor," talks excessively, blurts answers, can't wait turn, interrupts). Required for diagnosis [S1][S2]:
- **Symptom count:** ≥6 symptoms in a domain for children/adolescents <17; **≥5** for ages ≥17 (lower adult threshold).
- **Onset:** several symptoms present **before age 12**.
- **Pervasiveness:** symptoms in **≥2 settings** (home, school/work, social).
- **Impairment:** clear interference with social, academic, or occupational functioning; not better explained by another disorder.
- **Severity specifier:** mild / moderate / severe. DSM-5 also added a *partial remission* specifier for those who previously met full criteria.

**The three presentations [S1][S3]**
DSM-5/DSM-5-TR renamed the old "subtypes" as **presentations**, because they can shift over a person's life:
- **Combined** — meets both inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity thresholds (most common, esp. in referred children).
- **Predominantly Inattentive** — meets inattention threshold but not hyperactive-impulsive.
- **Predominantly Hyperactive/Impulsive** — meets hyperactive-impulsive but not inattention (most common in young children; rarer by adulthood).
A child diagnosed as Combined may present as Inattentive as an adult [S2][S3].

**Adults vs. children [S2]**
Overt hyperactivity tends to fade; in adults it becomes **inner restlessness** — feeling "driven by a motor," difficulty relaxing — rather than running/climbing. Inattention, disorganization, and impulsivity persist and dominate. Bright individuals may **compensate** until adult-life demands (career, parenting) overwhelm coping. CHADD stresses impairment in **≥2 major life settings** (e.g., job loss, financial/marital problems) as central, distinguishing clinical ADHD from everyday symptoms [S2].

**Gender: the missed inattentive/internalizing presentation in girls & women — VERIFIED [S3][S4]**
This claim is well supported:
- **NICE NG87 (Rec 1.2.2)** explicitly states ADHD "is thought to be under-recognised in girls and women," who are **less likely to be referred**, **more likely to have undiagnosed ADHD**, and **more likely to receive an incorrect diagnosis of another mental health or neurodevelopmental condition** [S4].
- Systematic review (Attoe & Climie, 2023): girls more often show the **inattentive** presentation (distraction, disorganization, forgetfulness), which is **less disruptive and less likely to trigger referral**; girls **internalize** more (anxiety, depression, emotional dysregulation) and externalize less, and often **mask** symptoms to meet feminine social norms. Result: later diagnosis and frequent prior treatment for anxiety/depression before ADHD is recognized [S3].
- **Sex ratio:** ~**3:1 (boys:girls)** in childhood clinical samples, narrowing toward ~**1:1 in adulthood** — evidence that girls are systematically under-identified, not less affected [S3].

**Coaching implications**
Inner restlessness, chronic disorganization, task-initiation avoidance, and "she's just anxious/lazy" histories can all signal under-recognized inattentive ADHD — especially in women diagnosed late. Coaching supports functioning but is not diagnostic; formal criteria require clinical assessment.

### [W1.3] Comprehensive ADHD symptom inventory — Confidence: high

ADHD is defined diagnostically by two symptom domains; everything else below is a clinically recognized *associated feature* a coach will see daily but which is not part of the formal criteria.

**1. Core diagnostic symptoms (DSM-5 / DSM-5-TR — unchanged between editions [S1]).** Two domains, each with 9 symptoms. Diagnosis requires ≥6 symptoms in a domain for children, but only **≥5 for ages 17+**, present **before age 12**, in **2+ settings** (e.g., home and work), with functional impairment [S2][S1]. Three presentations: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, or combined [S2].
- **Inattention:** misses details/careless errors; can't sustain attention on non-preferred tasks; "doesn't listen"; doesn't follow through / unfinished tasks; poor organization and sequencing; avoids sustained mental effort; loses things; easily distracted; forgetful in daily routines [S2].
- **Hyperactivity:** fidgets, can't stay seated, restless ("on the go"/driven by a motor), talks excessively; in adults often felt as internal restlessness rather than visible movement [S2].
- **Impulsivity:** blurts answers, can't wait turn, interrupts/intrudes — and broadly, acting without forethought [S2].

**2. Emotional dysregulation (a near-core feature — verify-this-claim resolved).** A systematic review concludes emotional dysregulation is a **core component of adult ADHD alongside inattention, hyperactivity and impulsiveness**, while cautioning it also appears in other disorders [S3]. It affects an estimated **34–70% of adults with ADHD** [S3]. A separate clinical review likewise calls it "a distinctive attribute of adult ADHD psychopathology" often misread as a mood disorder [S4]. Presents as short fuse, low frustration tolerance, rapid mood shifts, and intense reactions. *Coaching note:* normalize as wiring, not a character flaw; emotion-first responses derail plans.

**3. Rejection-sensitive dysphoria (RSD).** An intense emotional reaction to real or perceived rejection/criticism. It is a clinically described and lived-experience phenomenon, **not a DSM diagnosis**, and is best understood as one expression of emotional dysregulation [S4]. *Coaching note:* can drive avoidance, people-pleasing, and self-protective withdrawal.

**4. Executive-function & self-regulation features (Barkley/Brown framing).**
- **Time blindness:** weak felt-sense of time passing and of future deadlines ("now vs. not-now"); central to Barkley's model of ADHD as impaired self-regulation across time [S5]. *Coaching note:* externalize time (timers, visual calendars).
- **Working-memory deficits:** the "leaky bucket" — instructions and intentions drop out before use [S5]. *Coaching note:* offload to written/visual cues; one step at a time.
- **Hyperfocus:** episodic, intense absorption in stimulating tasks, with loss of time awareness — a strength to harness and a trap to manage [S5].

**5. Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome (CDS), formerly Sluggish Cognitive Tempo.** A 2022 expert work group (including Barkley) formalized the terminology change to CDS, judging it has "reached the threshold of recognition as a distinct syndrome" [S6]. Symptoms: daydreaminess, mental fogginess/"spaciness," staring, slow processing, lethargy/underactivity [S6]. It **overlaps with but is not identical to** ADHD inattention and co-occurs in roughly half of cases; its exact relationship to ADHD remains an open research question [S6]. *Coaching note:* looks like "lazy/zoned-out" but is a separate, low-energy/foggy profile.

### [W1.4] ADHD comorbidities & prevalence — Confidence: high

**Comorbidity is the rule, not the exception.** As many as **80% of adults with ADHD have at least one comorbid psychiatric disorder** [S1]. Coaches should assume a co-occurring condition is likely present and that it may be driving more day-to-day distress than the ADHD itself. Comorbidities complicate diagnosis, worsen prognosis, increase disease burden, and reduce treatment effectiveness [S1] — so a client "not responding" to strategies may have an untreated overlapping condition that needs clinical attention.

**Depression.** CHADD states "an adult affected by ADHD is **up to six times more likely** to also have depression than an adult without ADHD" [S2]. Note the hedge "up to six times" — this is a ceiling, not a typical multiplier. The figure traces to the National Comorbidity Survey Replication: major depression in 18.6% of adults with ADHD vs. 7.8% without; persistent depressive disorder 12.8% vs. 1.9%; bipolar 19.4% vs. 3.1% [S2]. A systematic review confirms elevated mood-disorder rates but reports wide ranges depending on sample (depression 8.6–55% in general-population ADHD samples; 15.4–39.7% in clinical samples) [S1].

**Anxiety.** ADHD and anxiety disorders carry roughly a **25% comorbidity rate** with each other across the lifespan [S3] — the figure to anchor on for general framing. In *adult clinical* ADHD samples, prevalence runs much higher and more variable (3.9–84% across studies, owing to differing tools and populations) [S1]. Practically: anxiety is extremely common, frequently under-recognized, and can masquerade as (or worsen) inattention and avoidance.

**Other high-frequency comorbidities** in adults with ADHD per the same review [S1]:
- **Substance use disorder** — often the single most frequent comorbidity (2.3–41.2% general population; 10.0–82.9% clinical).
- **Personality disorders** — 0.31–33.8% (general); 21.9–66% (clinical).
- **Bipolar disorder** — substantial overlap (see depression figures above).

**Autism (AuDHD) & shared genetics.** ADHD and autism co-occur frequently and share substantial genetic architecture: both are "**highly heritable (60–93%)**" per twin studies, with cross-disorder GWAS identifying seven shared and five differentiating genetic loci [S4]. For coaching, this validates the lived reality of AuDHD clients — the overlap is biological, not a client "failing" at one diagnosis. Expect blended profiles (e.g., ADHD novelty-seeking alongside autistic need for routine/predictability) and tailor accordingly.

**Coaching takeaways.** (1) Screen informally for low mood, anxiety, sleep problems, and substance use; (2) stay in lane — coaches do not diagnose or treat comorbidities, but should recognize red flags and refer to clinicians; (3) when strategies stall, consider an unaddressed comorbidity rather than client non-compliance; (4) coordinate with treating providers where possible.

### [W1.5] ADHD lifespan course & safety-critical risks — Confidence: high

**Lifespan course & persistence.** ADHD is a chronic, developmental condition, not a childhood-limited one. The World Federation of ADHD International Consensus Statement concludes that only about **one in six (≈17%) youths with ADHD still meet full diagnostic criteria at age 25, and about half show signs of residual impairment** [S1]. Coaching takeaway: most clients will not "outgrow" ADHD; symptoms shift (overt hyperactivity wanes, executive-function and inattention burdens persist), and impairment can outlast the formal diagnosis. Frame the work as lifelong self-regulation skill-building, not a temporary fix.

**Premature mortality (well-established).** In Dalsgaard et al.'s Danish nationwide cohort (1.92 million people; 32,061 with ADHD), people with ADHD had roughly **double the all-cause mortality of peers (adjusted mortality rate ratio 2.07, 95% CI 1.70–2.50)**, with **accidents the leading cause of death** [S2]. Excess risk was higher for those diagnosed in adulthood and for women. This is the strongest, most-cited mortality evidence — and it points coaches toward concrete safety scaffolding: driving habits, sleep, substance use, and impulsivity-driven accident risk.

**Estimated life-expectancy reduction (verify carefully — often distorted).** Barkley & Fischer (2019) followed **131 hyperactive and 71 control children to a mean age of 27** and entered **14 demographic/health/lifestyle variables into a life-expectancy calculator** to *estimate* remaining years [S3]. Childhood ADHD-C was associated with a **9.5-year reduction in healthy estimated life expectancy (ELE)** and an **8.4-year reduction in total ELE** vs. controls; **persistence of ADHD into adulthood was linked to a 12.7-year reduction in ELE** [S3]. Critical caveats for accurate coaching: these are **projected/estimated** figures from a calculator driven by *modifiable* mediators (low education, low income, smoking, alcohol, poor sleep, low exercise) — **not observed deaths**, and from one cohort. The authors explicitly stress the gap is "**not immutable**" and is largely a function of changeable lifestyle/health factors [S3]. Use this as motivation for health behavior change, never as a fixed sentence. Avoid the common viral framing "ADHD takes ~13 years off your life" without these qualifiers.

**Elevated suicide risk (well-established).** Septier et al.'s meta-analysis (57 studies) found ADHD significantly associated across the suicidal spectrum: **suicide attempts OR 2.37 (95% CI 1.64–3.43), suicidal ideation 3.53 (2.94–4.25), suicidal plans 4.54 (2.46–8.37), and completed suicide 6.69 (3.24–17.39)**; results held after adjusting for confounders [S4]. Coaching implication: suicide risk is real and elevated — coaches are **not** clinicians, so the rule is screen-and-refer. If a client signals hopelessness, self-harm, or suicidal thoughts, route to crisis/clinical care immediately and stay within coaching scope. Undiagnosed/untreated ADHD appears to carry particular risk [S5].

**Protective note.** Consensus evidence indicates ADHD medication treatment is associated with *reduced* risk of several adverse outcomes, including suicide [S1] — reinforcing that coaching should complement, not replace, clinical treatment.
