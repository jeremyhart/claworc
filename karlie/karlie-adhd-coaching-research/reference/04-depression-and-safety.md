### [W4.15] ADHD–depression comorbidity: epidemiology & mechanism — Confidence: high

**Prevalence (use ranges, not a single "headline" number)**
- Reported depression rates among adults with ADHD vary widely by setting and method: a systematic review found depressive-disorder prevalence of **8.6%–55% in ADHD groups vs 1.2%–12.5% in non-ADHD** (general-population samples), and 15.4%–39.7% vs 5.8%–39.6% in clinical samples — heterogeneity was too high to pool a single estimate [S1]. A narrative review similarly cites **18.6%–53.3%** among adults with ADHD [S2]. Bottom line for coaching: depression is several-fold more common with ADHD, but no clean "X%" figure is well-established — avoid quoting one as fact.
- In children/adolescents with ADHD, a meta-analysis (24 studies, N≈5,047) found a **pooled depression rate of 11.3%** (95% CI ~7–16%); case-control data showed **12% vs 2%** in neurotypical peers. Rates were higher in **females (20.9%) than males (9.0%)** [S3].
- A widely circulated "OR 4.5" figure for adult ADHD-depression appears in secondary summaries but I could not verify it against a primary meta-analysis — treat as **reported but unverified** [S2].

**Bidirectional / causal relationship (the strongest evidence)**
- A large genetically-informed study (Garcia-Argibay et al., 2024) used three designs. A Swedish sibling comparison (N≈1.02M) found ADHD carried a **~4-fold increased risk of later MDD (HR 4.12; 95% CI 3.62–4.69)** even after adjusting for shared genetics/family environment [S4].
- Mendelian randomization showed **genetic liability for ADHD causally raises MDD risk (OR 1.15; 95% CI 1.08–1.23)**, and the modeled direction was **ADHD → depression (unidirectional in this analysis)**, not the reverse [S4]. Coaching implication: ADHD often comes first developmentally and helps *drive* depression — treating/scaffolding ADHD is plausibly protective.

**How they compound each other (mechanism — coach-facing)**
- ADHD executive-function deficits (poor follow-through, disorganization, time-blindness) generate repeated failure, missed goals and reward "misses." Depression's **anhedonia and low drive** then deepen avoidance of exactly the activation/initiation that ADHD already makes hard — a downward loop [S2].
- Shared neurobiology: both involve **dopamine/norepinephrine dysregulation in reward circuitry (ventral striatum), reduced hedonic tone, and rumination/cognitive rigidity**, which is why low motivation and "can't get started" look similar in each [S2].

**Demoralization vs. clinical depression (important differential)**
- Chronic ADHD failure commonly produces **demoralization** — low self-esteem, frustration, "what's the point" feelings tied to specific setbacks — which can mimic depression but tends to **lift when circumstances/wins improve and is mood-reactive**, lacking the pervasive anhedonia, sustained low mood, and neurovegetative signs (sleep/appetite/worthlessness) of a major depressive episode [S2]. Karlie should normalize demoralization and rebuild momentum, but **escalate to clinical screening/referral** if low mood is persistent, non-reactive, or includes hopelessness/self-harm thoughts.

**Emotional-dysregulation overlap**
- Emotional dysregulation is **common in ADHD itself** (a major feature, not necessarily a sign of depression). Barkley argued deficient emotional self-regulation (DESR) is a **core component of ADHD** alongside behavioral/cognitive dysregulation [S5]; a meta-analysis (77 studies, N≈32,044) found impairment across emotional-regulation domains in ADHD [S5]. So irritability, rejection-sensitivity and mood swings can be **ADHD-driven** — don't assume they mean comorbid depression, but watch for sustained low mood that crosses the line.

- Demoralization-vs-MDD distinction here is synthesized from review-level mechanism discussion, not a single diagnostic instrument; any client showing persistent low mood, hopelessness, or self-harm ideation needs clinical assessment, not coaching alone.

### [W4.16] Behavioral Activation for depression — Confidence: high

**What the model is.** Behavioral Activation (BA) is a structured, present-focused behavioral treatment for depression. Its core premise: depression is maintained by a self-reinforcing cycle in which low mood drives withdrawal and avoidance, which removes sources of positive reinforcement and deepens low mood [S1][S2]. BA breaks this cycle from the *outside in* — change behavior first, and mood follows — rather than waiting for motivation to appear [S2]. The contemporary model (Martell, Dimidjian, and colleagues; Jacobson's lineage) treats depression *functionally*, asking what a behavior does for the person and what contingencies maintain it, rather than locating the problem inside the person [S1][S2].

**Core components (from Kanter et al.'s review of the model) [S2]:**
- **Activity monitoring** — tracking daily activities alongside mood/mastery/pleasure ratings to surface the behavior–mood link.
- **Activity scheduling** — planning specific, graded activities in advance ("outside-in" action), structured by time.
- **Values- and goals-based action** — selecting activities by what matters to the person and their longer-term goals, not just immediate symptom relief.
- **Targeting avoidance** — identifying overt *and* subtle avoidance, then scheduling approach behaviors as alternatives (often taught via the **TRAP → TRAC** frame: Trigger-Response-Avoidance-Pattern replaced by Trigger-Response-Alternative-Coping).
- **Regularizing routines** (sleep, meals, activity rhythm) and **redirecting rumination** outward toward engagement rather than internal focus.

**Evidence base.**
- Ekers et al. 2014 (meta-analysis, 26 RCTs, N≈1,524): BA was superior to control conditions with a **large effect, SMD −0.74 (95% CI −0.91 to −0.56; k=25, N=1,088)**, and modestly superior to antidepressant medication, **SMD −0.42 (95% CI −0.83 to −0.00; k=4, N=283)**. Caveat: study quality was low in most trials (only 7/26 met ≥3 quality standards) and heterogeneity was moderate (I²≈42%) [S3].
- COBRA trial (Richards et al. 2016, *Lancet*; non-inferiority RCT, N=440): BA was **non-inferior to CBT** at 12 months (mean PHQ-9 difference 0.1, 95% CI −1.3 to 1.5; margin 1.9). The authors concluded BA "can be delivered by junior mental health workers with less intensive and costly training, with no lesser effect than CBT" [S4].

**Coach-usable vs. clinical (important).**
- *Coach-usable surface structure*: activity monitoring, planning small valued/values-aligned actions, scheduling by time-of-day, the "act first, motivation follows" principle, routine regularization, and naming avoidance loops to substitute an approach action. These map cleanly onto ADHD coaching's action orientation and externalization of structure. BA's appeal for coaching is that its *frontline* version was designed to be deliverable by non-specialists [S4].
- *Clinical / out of scope for a coach*: treating a diagnosed major depressive episode, formal functional analysis as therapy, and any case with safety risk. BA is an evidence-based **treatment for depression** [S3]; a coach can borrow its activation *techniques* for low motivation/avoidance but must not position this as depression treatment, and should refer when depression is suspected or present.

### [W4.17] Coaching ADHD + depression together — Confidence: high

**Why these two co-occur, and what it means for coaching.** Depression is among the most common comorbidities in ADHD: reported prevalence ranges from **18.6% to 53.3%** in people with ADHD, and roughly **70% of adults with ADHD have at least one additional mental-health condition** [S1]. The AAP guideline likewise notes that the majority of youth with ADHD meet criteria for another disorder, with internalizing conditions (anxiety, depression) especially common in girls/women [S2]. Coaches should treat low motivation, withdrawal, and "I never finish anything" as overlapping ADHD-and-depression terrain, not pure character or pure ADHD.

**Sequencing (coach inside a treatment team; do not direct medical care).** Clinical consensus is to **prioritize the most severe, functionally impairing, and unstable condition first**, and for mood disorders this typically means stabilizing the depression (often pharmacologically) before reassessing residual ADHD symptoms; sequential rather than simultaneous changes help clarify what's working [S1][S2]. For the coach this means: pace the work to the client's current capacity, hold heavy executive-function/productivity goals lightly while mood is acute, and coordinate around (not ahead of) any prescriber. Note for hope-building: ADHD medication treatment is associated with *reduced* depression and suicide risk at the population level [S3] — useful psychoeducation, but a clinical decision, never a coaching recommendation.

**Working with fluctuating motivation/energy — lead with MI, then tiny-step activation.** Brief Behavioral Activation (BA) — scheduling small, values-linked, rewarding activities to interrupt withdrawal — has strong empirical support but **fails on adherence when motivation isn't addressed first** [S4]. The evidence-based move is to integrate Motivational Interviewing *before and throughout* activation [S4]:
- **Evoke, don't prescribe.** Have the client say *how* a between-session action might help them, and selectively reflect their own change talk rather than supplying reasons [S4].
- **Anchor in values.** Identify activities tied to what matters to them, and explore "what life might be like if they lived by those values" to build motivational pull [S4].
- **Make steps genuinely tiny and reinforce partial wins.** Use behavioral shaping: "partial completion is reinforced so long as it is a step toward the full assignment" [S4]. On low-energy days, shrink the step, don't skip the day.

**Anti-shame stance (this is the load-bearing skill).** In ADHD CBT work, homework non-completion is commonly driven by shame at not finishing, demoralization/depression, self-competency doubts, and an inability to summon the "energy of activation" — and shame can cause clients to skip sessions entirely [S5]. Counter this explicitly: **affirm any occasion the client did engage, ask what helped, and name personal strengths that contributed before touching obstacles** [S4]. Pair this with explicit respect for autonomy ("I can't make you do these — it's genuinely up to you"), which lowers defensiveness [S4].

**Expectation-setting.** Frame coaching as reducing impairment and rebuilding momentum, not "fixing" ADHD or curing depression — consensus is explicit that treatment reduces symptoms/risk rather than curing [S3]. Expect non-linear progress; build the relapse/low-day plan in advance.

**When to pause or refer out.** Coaching is not depression treatment. Refer to / defer to a clinician when: depression appears moderate–severe or worsening; there is any suicidal ideation, intent, or self-harm risk; the client is destabilizing; or the presentation exceeds your training/scope. Guideline language is direct: if not trained/experienced in diagnosis or initiating treatment, refer to an appropriate subspecialist [S2]. Have a crisis-escalation pathway agreed before you need it.

### [W4.18] Crisis, safety & scope-of-practice protocol — Confidence: high

A coach is **not** a therapist. ICF is explicit: "a coach does not diagnose nor does a coach offer treatment" [S1]. Coaching works with well-functioning people on the present and future; therapy addresses psychopathology, deep-seated emotional issues, and dysfunction [S1]. The single most important referral trigger is **daily functioning**: "Is the client's issue interfering with their daily functioning?" If self-care, work, or relationships are impaired for weeks, refer [S1]. This boundary is an ICF ethical obligation, not a preference — Code of Ethics §3.18 (set scope/limits up front) and §3.23 (refer to other professionals when warranted) [S1][S2].

**Warning signs that warrant referral** (watch for a *cluster* — duration, frequency, intensity — not one sign alone) [S1]:
- **Depression:** severe sadness/hopelessness or loss of interest >2 weeks; worthlessness, **recurrent thoughts of death or suicide** [S1].
- **Suicidal ideation / self-harm:** talking about hopelessness or "no reason to go on living"; giving away possessions; seeking means (e.g., buying a gun); recurrent thoughts of death; any attempt in the past 24 months [S1].
- Also: anxiety, eating disorders, PTSD/trauma, substance abuse, thought disorders (paranoia, delusions, hallucinations) [S1].

**A coach MUST:**
- Treat client safety as paramount over confidentiality; pre-define in the coaching agreement the situations where you would break confidentiality [S1].
- For **imminent danger** to self or others: phone local police/emergency services immediately; stay with the person until help arrives; ask what means of harm they have and get it given to someone for safekeeping; request a "wellness check"; listen without judging, arguing, or threatening [S1].
- If suicide is suspected but danger is not immediate: contact a crisis/suicide-prevention hotline and the client's local support services; you may also seek free brief consultation from a therapist [S1].
- Refer supportively (meet privately, name specific observed signs, offer options, keep a vetted referral list on file), and follow up — though the client may decline, and that is their right unless they are at risk of harm [S1].

**A coach MUST NOT:** diagnose, label, or treat mental illness; act as the client's therapist; "fix" the crisis alone; or keep coaching as if the issue isn't there. Feeling responsible for, overwhelmed by, or anxious about a client is itself a signal to consult a supervisor/mentor or refer [S1].

**Crisis resources (give the local one):**
- **US** — 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: call or text **988**, or chat at 988lifeline.org; 24/7 [S3].
- **NZ** — **1737** Need to Talk? (call/text, free, 24/7) [S4]; Lifeline Aotearoa **0800 543 354** or text **4357**; Suicide Crisis Helpline **0508 828 865** [S4].
- **AU** — Lifeline **13 11 14** (or text 0477 13 11 14); Beyond Blue **1300 22 4636**; emergency **000** [S5].
- **UK & ROI** — Samaritans **116 123** (free, 24/7) [S6].
- **International** — direct clients to a local line via findahelpline.com, Befrienders Worldwide, or the IASP crisis-centre directory [S1][S7]. Always know the client's **local emergency number** and region, since coaching is often remote.

**Gaps / needs human or clinical check:**
- The ICF guide is consensus expert opinion ("a pilot"), not a clinical instrument; it explicitly states the signs are "not meant to help diagnose." It is designed for developed English-speaking countries and may not fit other health systems/cultures — adapt accordingly.
- Crisis numbers change and vary by country; verify the current local line at point of use (e.g., via findahelpline.com). Karlie, as an AI, cannot place a 988/000/111 call or perform a wellness check — protocol must route the human to call, or surface the number, not attempt intervention itself.
- Jurisdiction-specific duty-to-warn / mandatory-reporting and confidentiality law is out of scope here and should be reviewed by a local professional and reflected in the coaching agreement.
