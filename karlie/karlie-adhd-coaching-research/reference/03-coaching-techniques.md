### [W3.11] Motivational Interviewing — Confidence: high

**What MI is.** Motivational Interviewing (MI) is "a person-centered counseling style for addressing the common problem of ambivalence about change" (Miller & Rollnick) [S1]. It is *guiding*, not directing or following: the coach evokes the client's own reasons for change rather than installing the coach's. The 4th edition (2023) frames the work as four overlapping **processes — Engaging, Focusing, Evoking, Planning** — and stresses using MI across the whole change process, not just at the start [S2]. For an ADHD client who feels nagged, lectured, or "broken," MI's stance is the antidote: the client is the expert on their own life and the source of the plan.

**The Spirit (PACE).** Technique without spirit underperforms; clinicians who score high on relational/spirit items produce larger client outcomes than those high on technique alone [S1].
- **Partnership** — active collaboration; you influence, the client drives [S1].
- **Acceptance** — absolute worth, accurate empathy, **autonomy support**, and affirmation [S1]. (Autonomy support matters acutely in ADHD, where demand-resistance is common.)
- **Compassion** — prioritizing the client's welfare over your agenda [S1].
- **Evocation** — drawing out the motivation, values, and strengths the client already has [S1].

**Core skills (OARS).** Open questions (invite elaboration), Affirmations (genuine appreciation of strengths/effort), Reflective listening (hypothesize the underlying meaning and reflect it back — the workhorse skill), and Summaries (gather key themes and reinforce change) [S1]. The 4th edition adds nuance: simple vs. complex affirmations, and strategic directional questions [S2].

**Ambivalence & change talk.** Ambivalence is normal, not denial or resistance [S1]. Listen for and selectively reinforce **change talk (DARN-CAT)** — Desire, Ability, Reasons, Need (preparatory) and Commitment, Activation, Taking steps (mobilizing) — while not amplifying **sustain talk** [S1]. Higher change talk predicts better outcomes; higher sustain talk predicts worse [S1]. Practically: ask evocative questions ("What would be good about getting your meds going?"), reflect change talk when it appears, then summarize it back.

**Rolling with resistance (discord).** MI reframes "resistance" as a signal about the *relationship in the moment*, not client pathology: "If discord arises… change direction or listen more carefully" [S1]. Respond with simple/amplified/double-sided reflections, reframing, and explicit emphasis on autonomy — never argue for change (the "righting reflex" backfires) [S1]. This is directly useful with ADHD pushback and with depression-linked low motivation, where confrontation deepens disengagement.

**Evidence base — calibrated.** General behavior change: a large overview of **104 systematic reviews (39 meta-analyses)** found mainly short-term (<6 months), *small* but significant benefits, strongest for reducing binge/heavy drinking, substance use in dependency, and increasing physical activity; only 7% of comparisons reached moderate-quality evidence and ~83% were low/very-low quality — "more high quality research is needed" [S3]. ADHD-specific: in a community RCT of MI-enhanced behavior therapy (STAND) for **278 adolescents**, MI did **not** beat usual care on ADHD-symptom outcomes, but improved **engagement** — greater parent participation (d=0.88) and markedly better medication engagement (OR≈7.18 at follow-up) [S4]. Takeaway for Karlie: treat MI as a high-value *engagement and adherence* tool and a relational stance, not a standalone cure for ADHD symptoms.

### [W3.12] GROW model & ICF coaching competencies — Confidence: high

**The GROW model — structure and origin.** GROW is a four-stage conversation framework — **G**oal, **R**eality, **O**ptions, **W**ill — developed in the UK in the late 1980s by Sir John Whitmore with colleagues Graham Alexander and Alan Fine; Whitmore first published it in *Coaching for Performance* (1992) [S1][S2]. It was directly influenced by Timothy Gallwey's *Inner Game* method: Gallwey, a tennis coach, found that telling players what to fix rarely produced lasting change, so he used questions to help them discover corrections themselves. GROW inherits this **non-prescriptive, self-discovery stance** — the coach asks, the client finds the answer [S1]. Note the "W" ambiguity: Whitmore insisted W = **Will** (the will/commitment to act), while co-developers used "Way Forward" or "Wrap-up." For an ADHD coach, Whitmore's "Will" is the load-bearing one — commitment and accountability are exactly where ADHD clients struggle [S1][S2].

**Running the four stages (practical, non-linear).** Move between stages as insight emerges — it is not a rigid script [S2].
- **Goal:** "What would you like to achieve? What will success look like? Why does this matter now?" [S2]
- **Reality:** "What's happening right now? What have you already tried? What's getting in the way? What assumptions might you be holding?" [S2]
- **Options:** "What could you do? What else? What would you try if obstacles weren't an issue? Who could support you?" [S2]
- **Will:** "What will you do first? On a 1–10 scale, how committed are you? What might derail you and how will you respond? How will you hold yourself accountable?" [S2]

**Whitmore's foundation: awareness + responsibility.** Whitmore framed these as the two pillars of coaching — *awareness* lets people see what is really going on; *responsibility* gives them the power to act on it. GROW only works when questions raise both; otherwise it becomes a checklist [S2][S4]. This is the core mechanism a coach is actually selling.

**ICF coaching stance — the 8 Core Competencies (2019 model, four domains).** ICF consolidated 11 competencies into **8**, effective 2019 [S3][S5]:
- *A. Foundation:* 1) Demonstrates Ethical Practice; 2) Embodies a Coaching Mindset.
- *B. Co-Creating the Relationship:* 3) Establishes & Maintains Agreements; 4) Cultivates Trust & Safety; 5) Maintains Presence.
- *C. Communicating Effectively:* 6) Listens Actively; 7) Evokes Awareness.
- *D. Cultivating Learning & Growth:* 8) Facilitates Client Growth [S3][S5].

**Key competencies for Karlie's stance.**
- **Client-led agenda / partnering:** ICF stresses client autonomy — partner with the client to set goals and design actions; the *client* owns the agenda and the choices [S5]. Karlie should surface options, not prescribe them.
- **Listens Actively (6):** attend to the client's full system — words, emotions, body language, nonverbal cues — not just content [S5].
- **Evokes Awareness (7):** this competency merged the old "powerful questioning," "direct communication," and "creating awareness." It is about provoking new understanding via open questions, observation, and shared insight — *the* engine of a coaching conversation [S3]. Pairs naturally with GROW's awareness goal.
- **Facilitates Client Growth (8):** translate insight into action and **build accountability systems** [S5] — the ICF analogue of GROW's "Will."

### [W3.13] Solution-Focused techniques for coaching — Confidence: high

**Origins & stance.** Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) was developed by social workers Steve de Shazer and Insoo Kim Berg with their team at the Brief Family Therapy Center in Milwaukee in the late 1970s–1980s, building on the brief-therapy tradition of Watzlawick, Weakland & Fisch [S1]. Its core wager: the causes of problems can be complex, but solutions need not address those causes directly. The coach takes a "not-knowing" stance, treats the client as the expert on their own life, assumes change is constant and inevitable, and works to "do more of what already works" [S1]. This maps cleanly onto ADHD coaching, where the goal is durable behaviour change, not diagnosis or insight.

**The core toolkit (coach-facing).**
- **Scaling questions** — the "work horses" of the approach [S1]. "On 0–10, where are you on getting morning meds consistent?" Then: "What put you at a 4 and not a 2?" (surfaces existing assets) and "What would a 5 look like — what's one thing you'd be doing?" (defines a concrete, small next step). Useful for tracking progress between sessions and making invisible gains visible.
- **The Miracle Question** — improvised by Berg in the early 1980s for a client who couldn't name a goal [S1]. "Suppose overnight a miracle solved this — what's the first small thing you'd notice tomorrow that tells you it happened?" Builds a vivid, behaviourally specific preferred future. Keep it concrete and pull for observable signs.
- **Exception-finding** — "Tell me about a recent day this went better than usual. What was different? What did *you* do?" Identifies the client's own working strategies to amplify [S1].
- **Strengths/resources & compliments** — genuine, evidence-based affirmation of effort, resilience and what the client is already managing; pair with small between-session experiments ("do more of what works") rather than prescriptive homework [S1].
- **Coping questions** (for low moments): "How have you kept going at all?" — finds resourcefulness inside struggle [S1].

**Evidence base.** A 2024 meta-analysis (72 studies, 489 effect sizes) found a large overall effect of SFBT on psychosocial outcomes (Hedges *g* = 1.17), with larger effects in non-clinical samples (*g* = 1.50) than clinical (*g* = 0.78), and larger effects for group (*g* = 1.64) than individual delivery (*g* = 0.48) [S2]. Note: such large effects partly reflect weak control conditions and analog/non-clinical samples, so treat them as encouraging rather than definitive.

For **coaching mechanisms specifically**, two experimental studies are directly useful. Solms et al. (2022), with 183 medical residents/PhD students, found solution-focused prompts produced significantly higher positive affect (hedonic tone) and approach-goal orientation and lower tense arousal than problem-focused prompts — but **no** difference in self-efficacy or number/quality of action steps [S3]. Neipp et al. (2021) found miracle and exception questions reduced negative affect most, while scaling questions generated the most concrete action steps [S4]. Practical takeaway: use miracle/exception questions to shift mood and motivation; use scaling to convert that into next actions.

**Gaps / needs human or clinical check:**
- SFBT's evidence base is in **therapy**, not ADHD coaching specifically; no ADHD-population RCT of SFBT techniques was located. Effects on coaching outcomes are inferred from analog/single-session experiments, not longitudinal coaching trials.
- The two coaching experiments used single-session writing tasks (medical/student samples), so durability and transfer to real coaching relationships are unestablished.
- Coaches must stay within scope: SFBT in coaching is for goals/behaviour, not treatment of co-occurring depression, anxiety or trauma — refer to a clinician when those are present.

### [W3.14] CBT-informed & behavioral techniques within coaching scope — Confidence: high

**Why these techniques belong in coaching.** For adults with ADHD, structured psychological/behavioral support is the recommended non-drug approach. NICE NG87 advises a "structured supportive psychological intervention focused on ADHD" with regular follow-up, and states treatment "may involve elements of or a full course of CBT" [S1]. The active ingredients in evidence-based CBT for adult ADHD are explicitly skills-based: "specific strategies — both cognitive and behavioral — to facilitate time awareness, prioritizing, scheduling, tracking, and overcoming distraction and procrastination" [S2]. Many of these map cleanly onto coaching's action/accountability remit.

**Coach-usable techniques (drawn from manualized CBT/MCT protocols):**
- **Self-monitoring** — externalize executive function: planners/calendars, time-awareness checks, tracking tasks and follow-through. Core to Solanto's Meta-Cognitive Therapy (MCT) curriculum [S3][S2].
- **Activity scheduling & planning** — prioritizing, scheduling, breaking ("dismantling") tasks into manageable parts, and goal flow-charting [S3].
- **Contingency / reward** — contingent self-reward and visualizing long-term reward to bridge ADHD's delay-aversion and weak future-orientation [S3].
- **Problem-solving** — anticipatory troubleshooting of obstacles before they derail a plan [S2].
- **Cognitive reframing (light touch)** — noticing and gently challenging self-defeating, all-or-nothing thoughts ("I always fail at this") that block task initiation. Note: in the clinical protocols this targets "depressogenic and anxiogenic automatic thoughts" [S3] — coaches reframe around tasks/habits, not mood disorders (see boundary).

**Evidence these work.** In Solanto's RCT (n=88), MCT beat supportive therapy on blind-rated inattention by 2.7 points (95% CI 0.9–4.6; p<0.005), with a responder odds ratio of 5.41 (95% CI 1.77–16.55) [S3]. Across the broader CBT-for-adult-ADHD literature, two landmark 2010 trials using blinded structured assessments showed effect sizes of 0.52 and 0.58 on core symptoms [S2]. (Larger effect sizes versus waitlist, ~1.0, partly reflect weak controls; blinded/active-control estimates are the honest ones [S2].)

**The scope-of-practice boundary (critical for Karlie).** Coaching applies these techniques to skill-building, structure, and goal achievement in the *present and future*; it does not diagnose, treat mental-health conditions, or process trauma. PAAC's standard is explicit: "ADHD coaching complements clinical care. It does not replace it," and PAAC coaches "work alongside physicians, psychologists, therapists, and educational professionals as part of a broader system of care" [S4]. NICE frames the structured psychological/CBT intervention as something delivered within healthcare for ADHD, not as generic life advice [S1].

**Where coaching technique becomes therapy — refer out when:**
- Reframing surfaces clinical depression, anxiety, trauma, suicidality, or persistent hopelessness (these need a licensed clinician, not a coach) [S4].
- The "automatic thoughts" being targeted are mood-disorder cognitions rather than task/habit beliefs [S3].
- The client wants medication review, diagnosis, or treatment of a co-occurring condition — out of coaching scope [S4].
Practical rule: coaches use *cognitive-behavioral techniques* (self-monitoring, scheduling, reward, problem-solving, task-focused reframing) but do not deliver *cognitive-behavioral therapy*; when emotional or psychiatric content dominates, partner with or refer to a therapist/clinician [S4][S2].
